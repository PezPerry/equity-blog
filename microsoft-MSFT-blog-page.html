A Brilliant Business, Just Not Cheap Enough

All figures in US dollars. Microsoft reports in USD with a fiscal year ending 30 June; the tear sheet shows earnings per share in cents (1,364 = $13.64).

Briefing

  • Thesis: Microsoft is a genuinely excellent business — stable mid-teens revenue growth (FY 2025 up 14.9% to $281.7 billion), software-grade gross margins of 68.8%, and pre-tax profit growing at roughly 18.5% per annum over five years. But quality and value are different things, and at the current price the stock screens as approximately 21% overvalued.
  • Valuation view: Using FY 2025 earnings per share of $13.64 with an 18% growth rate, a 9x multiple, and a 5% valuation uplift, I arrive at a valuation of $337.71. Against a current price of $427.34, that's a value opportunity of −21.0%.
  • Key risk: The capital expenditure programme is staggering — guidance of roughly $190 billion for FY 2026, up from $32 billion in FY 2025. The returns on this AI infrastructure spend are still to be proven.
  • Key strength: Microsoft isn't locked into any single AI provider. Azure AI Foundry offers 11,000+ models and is used by around 80% of the Fortune 500. Unlike Apple, which is tied to specific hardware cycles, Microsoft sits as a software layer that can integrate whichever AI is best at any moment.
  • Overall stance: Overvalued — no action. A wonderful company at an uncomfortable price.

Business Model

Microsoft is one of the world's largest technology companies, operating across three reporting segments: Productivity & Business Processes (Microsoft 365, LinkedIn, Dynamics), Intelligent Cloud (Azure, server products, enterprise services), and More Personal Computing (Windows, gaming including Activision Blizzard, search and devices).

The business model is built on deep integration into corporate and personal computing — operating systems, productivity software, cloud infrastructure, and increasingly artificial intelligence tooling. This breadth is the core of the investment case: Microsoft isn't dependent on any single product or hardware cycle, which makes it unusually resilient compared with more concentrated technology peers.

Turnover and Profit History

Microsoft has seen reasonably stable turnover growth over the last several years, with the exception of FY 2023, which only saw a 6.9% revenue rise. FY 2024 saw 15.7% and FY 2025 saw 14.9%. The three years before that were strong too — FY 2020 at 13.6%, FY 2021 at 17.5% as COVID flowed through, and FY 2022 at 18.0%. So the company continues to see good levels of turnover growth.

🎓 Clarity Why does Microsoft's lack of "lock-in" matter? Azure AI Foundry gives Microsoft access to more than 11,000 AI models from OpenAI, Cohere, DeepSeek, Meta, Mistral, xAI and others — and around 80% of Fortune 500 companies already use it. Because Microsoft sits as a software and infrastructure layer rather than depending on one piece of hardware or one AI model, it can plug in whichever provider is best at any given moment. Compare this with a hardware-led company like Apple, which must make each device the leading premium product and is more likely to tie itself to a single AI provider. That flexibility is a genuine competitive advantage in a fast-moving AI landscape.

Capital Expenditure — the Concern

FY 2025 capital expenditure of $32 billion was high — about 11.4% of revenue. But for FY 2026, Microsoft has guided towards roughly $190 billion of capital spending — a colossal step up, well above Wall Street estimates, driven partly by soaring memory prices. This is the area I find concerning: the AI capex burden is enormous, and the returns on it are still to be proven. But I do think Microsoft is likely to find opportunities towards its own profitability within these areas.

Microsoft vs Apple — the Adaptability Argument

A key part of my thinking on the probability weighting: I am not particularly expecting things to be outside the norm for Microsoft. They are not dependent on single pieces of hardware or technology. They are integrated into so many different areas of so many people's lives — unlike, say, Apple, which needs to ensure each model it releases is the leading premium device.

Microsoft, as more of a software and operating-system layer, doesn't have that same issue. I think they will be more adaptive to the AI shift: you can integrate almost any AI platform into Microsoft's services. In the shorter term, Apple is very likely to end up tied to a specific provider and focus on that, whereas Microsoft is likely to use whichever is best at any given moment. They are not locked in. I don't have particularly strong hopes for their own home-grown AI, but the lack of lock-in is an advantage.

Valuation

Assuming a longer-term growth rate of 18% — higher than their existing earnings per share growth, but more in keeping with their two-year and five-year average growth, and reflecting the AI integration tailwinds — with a valuation multiple of 9x and a 5% valuation uplift, I arrive at a valuation of $337.71 per share.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Capex burden: Roughly $190 billion of guided FY 2026 capital spending — a near-sixfold increase on FY 2025. If the AI returns don't materialise, this destroys value on a colossal scale.
  • Valuation: At 31.2x trailing earnings, the stock prices in a lot of future growth. Any disappointment in the growth rate or margin would hit the share price hard.
  • Growth deceleration: Revenue growth, while strong at 14.9%, is "ever so slightly lacking" for a company of this ambition. At this scale, sustaining high growth becomes progressively harder.
  • Debt likely to rise: Long-term debt fell to $14.15 billion in FY 2025, but the scale of capex ahead means this reduction is unlikely to continue — borrowing will probably rise again.
  • Home-grown AI uncertainty: Microsoft's own AI models are not a particular strength. The strategy relies on integrating third-party providers — which works while it has access, but is not fully within its control.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Share price below $340: At or below my valuation, the stock would become genuinely interesting given the quality of the business.
  • Evidence of capex payoff: Clear demonstration that the AI infrastructure spend is generating incremental, high-margin revenue would justify a higher multiple and a higher growth assumption.
  • Re-acceleration of revenue growth above 18%: If Azure and AI tooling push group growth back above the high-teens, the current price would look more justified.
  • Capex discipline: Any signal that the $190 billion guidance is being tempered toward returns rather than raw scale would reduce the key risk.
  • Resumption of buybacks: Meaningful share count reduction would support earnings per share growth and signal management confidence in the cash position.

Bottom Line — Overvalued

In an overall sense, I think Microsoft is overvalued — but not crazily so. There are a lot of positives: growth continues, margins are excellent, profit is coming in very nicely. The concerning area is the capital expenditure that will come through over the coming years in the AI race, though I expect them to find profitability opportunities within it.

The growth profile is great. But given the roughly 21% overvaluation I'm arriving at, it remains too expensive for me at this price. The company is likely to do fine into the medium term — it's simply not cheap enough.

Value opportunity: −21.0%. Probability: 50%.
