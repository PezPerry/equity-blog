1 Briefing

Debenhams Group — formerly Boohoo Group, now trading under the ticker DEBS following its rebrand on 31 March 2025 — has published its full-year results for the year ending 28 February 2026. The headline metrics contain genuine positives, and I want to give credit where it is due: gross margin is up 40 basis points to 51.1%, the first improvement since 2022.

2 Business Model

Debenhams Group operates as a portfolio of online fashion and lifestyle brands, anchored by the boohoo brand, which is now the largest in the group by GMV. The group’s strategic pivot centres on building the Debenhams name as a marketplace platform — a digital department store hosting partner brands — rather than purely as a direct retailer.

The Debenhams marketplace model sits at the heart of the investment case the company is trying to make. With 25,000 partner brands now live, up from 15,000, and 5.7 million active customers, there is evidently some traction. The marketplace has expanded into the US via Macy’s, Bloomingdale’s, Nordstrom, and Amazon Fashion. Operationally, the group has closed its UK distribution centre following years of development and is subletting the facility to ID Logistics on the remaining 8.5 years of the lease.

I understand the logic of trying to build a brand network around a central platform — it makes sense in principle to use established recognition to aggregate third-party demand and improve economics. I just remain sceptical about whether Debenhams, as a brand, carries sufficient consumer pull to make people choose it over Amazon, which is both a distribution partner for the US launch and the most obvious competitive threat.

3 What Matters for This Company

The central tension in this set of results — and the thing that genuinely matters for the investment case — is the relationship between margins and revenues. The company has demonstrated it can improve its profitability metrics. What it has not demonstrated is that it can do so while also growing, or even stabilising, its top line. Sales are falling sharply.

“A turnaround requires evidence that a business can both sell more and do so more profitably. Debenhams Group has shown one half of that equation in isolation.”
