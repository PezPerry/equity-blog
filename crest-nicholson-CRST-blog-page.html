Business Model

Crest Nicholson is a UK-based residential housebuilder, operating primarily across the south of England and the Midlands. The company is structured into five regional divisions: Midlands and Yorkshire, Southwest, South, Chiltern, and Eastern. It builds a mix of open-market homes, affordable housing, and bulk/private rented sector units.

Under its "Project Elevate" strategy, the company is transitioning away from lower-margin brownfield and bulk sites towards mid-premium owner-occupier housing. This repositioning is being led by CEO Martyn Clark, previously the chief commercial officer at Persimmon, who joined in June 2024. The company has approximately 40 active development outlets across England.

The company's headquarters are in Addlestone, Surrey, and it has been building homes for over 60 years. It holds a 5-star rating from the Home Builders Federation for customer satisfaction.

  • Banking covenant: The company is seeking temporary banking covenant relaxation. This is the most critical near-term issue.
  • Land sales collapse: Prospective land purchasers have softened their appetite. Only one land sale has been completed so far, and guidance has been slashed from £75–100 million to just £40 million. This is the single biggest driver of the profit warning.

  • Cost inflation: The Middle East conflict is pushing up energy costs, feeding through to build cost inflation of 1–2% on top of the previously guided 1–2%.
  • Property inventory backing: The company holds over £1 billion of property inventory against £99 million of interest-bearing debt. This is a liquidity/covenant issue, not a solvency issue.
  • No fire-selling: Importantly, the company is not seeing material levels of discounting and has not increased incentives. Trading in Midlands, Southwest, and Eastern divisions remains positive.
🎓 Clarity What is a banking covenant and why does a breach matter? When a company borrows money, the lender typically imposes conditions (covenants) that the company must meet — for example, maintaining a minimum ratio of profits to interest costs (interest coverage). If the company's profits fall below this threshold, it is in "breach" of the covenant. This doesn't mean the company is bankrupt — it means the bank has the right to demand immediate repayment or impose additional conditions. In practice, banks often agree to temporary relaxations (waivers), but these usually come with higher fees and tighter terms.

The Banking Covenant Issue

The company says it is "in the early stages of seeking temporary banking covenant relaxation." Based on the revised figures, the issue is most likely the interest coverage ratio. With EBIT of £5–15 million and interest costs of approximately £15 million, the coverage ratio is between 0.3x and 1.0x. A typical minimum covenant would require 2–3x coverage.

This is frustrating — the company has come out with this disclosure without explaining what specific covenant they're breaching or what relaxation they're seeking.

The company does have significant tangible asset backing (£1,056 million of property inventory against £99 million of interest-bearing loans), so this is a liquidity/covenant issue rather than a solvency issue. But they are at the mercy of their banks, and there will likely be additional costs associated with any waiver or amendment.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Banking covenant breach: The company is at the mercy of its lenders. If banks refuse to grant a waiver or impose onerous terms, it could force asset sales at distressed prices or trigger a rights issue.
  • Land sales timing: Only one land sale completed so far. If prospective purchasers continue to hold back, cash generation will remain under severe pressure.
  • Net debt trajectory: From £38 million to £100–120 million in a single year is alarming. Further deterioration could make the covenant situation worse.
  • Management quality: This company has had a poor reputation for management. They have been paying dividends while debt rises — this is questionable capital allocation.
  • Cost inflation escalation: If the Middle East conflict worsens, energy-linked material costs could rise further, squeezing already thin margins.
  • Sector contagion: A sharp selloff in one housebuilder can affect sentiment across the sector, potentially reducing appetite for land purchases from Crest's portfolio.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Covenant waiver secured on reasonable terms: This would remove the most immediate uncertainty and allow the company to focus on the turnaround.
  • Land sales resuming: Even one or two meaningful land disposals would significantly improve the cash position and reduce net debt concerns.
  • Takeover approach: The company has already refused two buyout proposals at 273p. At 66.5p, it is an even more compelling acquisition target — the property inventory alone is worth a multiple of the current market cap.
  • Open-market sales rate above 0.6: Sustained improvement in the sales rate would signal that the mid-premium repositioning is working and volume recovery is underway.
  • Dividend cancellation: Paradoxically, cutting the dividend would be a positive signal — it would show management is prioritising balance sheet repair over shareholder optics.

Conclusion — Buy

I think Crest Nicholson are in for quite a ride during 2026. There are some mega issues here — how has this situation arisen so quickly and dramatically, and at the same time as they are paying dividends? This is poor by management.

However, I still see that there is significant value being overlooked in the company at this current share price. There is over £1 billion of property inventory, interest-bearing debt of only £99 million, and net assets of £718 million. If another housebuilder were looking to build up their land and work-in-progress inventory, Crest Nicholson represents a compelling acquisition target — the value is transferable.
