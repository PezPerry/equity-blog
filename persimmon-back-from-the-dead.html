Briefing

Persimmon Homes (PSN) is one of the UK's largest housebuilders by volume. The share price has been hammered over the past couple of years, dropping from highs around 2,800p to roughly 1,050p today. The market has punished the entire sector on the back of higher mortgage rates, the cladding scandal, and general demand uncertainty.

But I think the sell-off has gone too far for Persimmon specifically, and the current price offers a genuinely attractive entry point. This is not a blind sectoral call — it is a company-specific thesis built on remediation progress, land bank optionality, and a credible volume recovery path if rates cooperate.

Business Model

Persimmon is a volume housebuilder — they build and sell new homes, predominantly to first-time buyers and families. Their model is straightforward: acquire land, obtain planning permission, build houses, sell them. They have a large strategic land bank, which is a key competitive advantage in a planning-constrained market.

Uniquely among large-cap peers, Persimmon owns Brickworks Ltd and operates a timber frame manufacturing operation, giving them a degree of vertical integration that Taylor Wimpey and Barratt largely lack. This provides some insulation from supply chain disruption and, in theory, margin support when input costs spike.

Operating margins have compressed sharply — from a peak of around 28 per cent down to roughly 14 per cent today. This reflects both cost inflation and a slower sales rate environment. The recovery of even half that margin compression would be highly meaningful for earnings.

What Matters for This Company

  • ▲ Mortgage rates & affordability. If the Bank of England cuts rates through 2026 and into 2027 as currently expected, affordability improves and transaction volumes should recover. Persimmon's heavy exposure to the first-time buyer segment makes any government support scheme — such as a Help to Buy replacement — a direct and leveraged tailwind.
  • ▲ Build quality remediation progress. Persimmon suffered serious reputational damage from widely reported build quality failures. The company has spent heavily to fix legacy issues and has overhauled construction processes and oversight. The market still applies a discount for this history, but I believe the worst is behind them — meaning the discount should narrow over time.
  • ▲ Land bank conversion & planning reform. The strategic land bank of roughly 80,000 plots is enormous, but its value depends on planning approvals and market conditions. The new planning reform legislation, if effective, should accelerate consents and bring more of this optionality into the money.

The intersection of rate cuts, reduced remediation drag, and planning reform represents a three-factor recovery thesis. Each factor independently improves the outlook; together, they could drive a meaningful re-rating.

Clarity

The key uncertainty is around the pace of volume recovery. Management guided for around 10,800 completions this year — modest growth. There is genuine upside to this number if mortgage rates fall faster than expected, but equally there could be downside if the housing market stalls again. Rate sensitivity is the single biggest variable in the near-term model.

The dividend picture adds a further layer of nuance. Persimmon cut its special dividend and has reduced the regular dividend. While cash generation remains solid, the balance sheet needs to fund ongoing remediation work and continued land investment. I would not approach this as an income position — this is a capital appreciation story, and investors expecting a chunky yield are likely to be disappointed in the near term.

  • ▼ Bear case: Rates stay higher for longer, transaction volumes stagnate, and remediation costs come in above guidance. Margins stay suppressed and the land bank remains underutilised. Share price could revisit lows.
  • ▲ Bull case: Rate cuts accelerate, government introduces meaningful FTB support, planning reform unlocks land, and margins recover toward 20 per cent. Significant earnings upgrades follow and the stock re-rates substantially above our MAEP target.
  • ▼ Structural risk: Any resurgence of cladding-related legal exposure or wider building safety legislation changes could create material, hard-to-quantify liabilities. This remains a tail risk worth monitoring.

Valuation

We apply the MAEP (Multiple-Adjusted Earnings Projection) method. The inputs and the derivation of our target price are set out below.

MAEP Target Price ~1,232p 85 × 1.15 × 12 × 1.10 × 0.85

Against the current price of approximately 1,050p, the MAEP target of ~1,232p implies roughly 17 per cent upside. This is not a screaming buy — there is no margin of safety so large that we can be cavalier about the risks outlined above. But 17 per cent upside, combined with a credible and multi-factor recovery trajectory, is sufficient to warrant accumulation at current levels.

The risk/reward is asymmetric in a way that favours patient investors. The downside is bounded by a large and relatively liquid land bank; the upside, if the three-factor thesis plays out, could comfortably exceed our MAEP target. We are not modelling that bull case — we are pricing only a measured, reasonable recovery.
