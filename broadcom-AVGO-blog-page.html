Business Model

Broadcom Inc is one of the largest semiconductor companies in the world, and it has expanded heavily into infrastructure software. Its semiconductors serve computing and networking, and custom AI accelerators now account for a huge amount of the business. It holds some manufacturing in house, but it is primarily a fabless designer. In software, it sells virtualisation, infrastructure and security software to large enterprises, financial institutions and governments.

🎓 Clarity What is a "fabless" designer? A fabless semiconductor company designs its chips but outsources the actual manufacturing to specialist foundries (fabrication plants, or "fabs", such as TSMC). That keeps capital spending far lower than owning factories, and is one reason companies like Broadcom can run such high gross margins. And a custom AI accelerator is a chip designed to a specific customer's requirements — typically a hyperscale cloud company — to run artificial-intelligence workloads, rather than a general-purpose processor sold off the shelf.

Revenue Growth

The revenue record is astounding. Growth of 14.9% (FY2021), 21.0% (FY2022), a lacklustre 7.9% (FY2023), then 44.0% (FY2024) and 23.9% (FY2025). From FY2021 to FY2025 turnover grew by around 132% — over four years, an average of roughly 33% a year. Remarkable levels. It is interesting that FY2025 was lower than FY2024, and worth noting that these growth rates are not very stable: they are mostly massive figures, but not stably in the same direction.

Gross margins have oscillated too — from 61.4% (FY2021) up to 68.9% (FY2023), landing at 67.8% in FY2025 — a surprising amount of movement, but always very high, as you would expect of a company of this nature. FY2025 operating profit was monumental at $26.1 billion, producing pre-tax profit of $23.3 billion and reported earnings per share of 477 cents. As always with these very large super-tech companies, the figures are monumental.

The operating leverage is striking: revenue up 23.9%, but operating income up 89% and net income nearly quadrupling, as the FY2024 VMware integration costs and amortisation fell away. Selling, general and administrative costs actually dropped −15.1%. This is the year the VMware acquisition stopped being a drag and started paying off.

🎓 Clarity What is operating leverage — and why did profit quadruple on 24% more revenue? Operating leverage describes what happens when revenue grows faster than costs: each extra dollar of sales carries more of itself through to profit. In Broadcom's case the effect was amplified by one-offs falling away — FY2024 was weighed down by the costs of integrating VMware (the huge virtualisation-software business Broadcom acquired) and by amortisation, which is the gradual expensing of the value of acquired assets. With those drags gone and costs actually falling, a 23.9% revenue rise became an 89% operating-income rise and a near-quadrupling of net income. The flip side: comparisons against a depressed year flatter the growth rate — reported earnings per share of 123 cents in FY2024 was itself an artificially low base.

Research & Development — History and Outlook

Research and development spending was broadly flat around $4.9–5.3bn for years, then jumped 77% in FY2024 when VMware was consolidated, and rose a further 17.9% in FY2025. So the FY2025 rise to $11.0bn is a continuation of the VMware step-change, not a fresh surge.

In Q1 FY2026 (quarter ended 1 February 2026), R&D rose 32% year on year — a faster rate than the full-year FY2025 increase — as the company pours investment into custom AI accelerators and AI networking. On that Q1 run-rate, full-year FY2026 R&D would plausibly land in the region of $13–14bn. The strategic logic is the AI-accelerator roadmap: Broadcom guided Q1 FY2026 AI semiconductor revenue to roughly double year on year to $8.2bn, and that kind of custom-silicon design work is R&D-intensive. So I expect R&D to keep climbing in absolute terms, even if it stays roughly flat as a percentage of revenue (it was 17% of revenue in FY2025, down from 18%).

What Matters for This Company

The company is growing very fast, in revenue terms and — especially in the last twelve months — on earnings per share. But the valuation rests on extrapolating a historical four-year earnings-per-share growth average of around 33.5%, and I do not think it will continue at quite that pace. Some will find that a ridiculous thing to doubt when the record is this strong, but the growth is not stable year to year.

They might well see explosive growth over the next three years, but I do not foresee sustained earnings-per-share growth faster than the low-thirties percent. To justify the market's valuation you would have to hope for something like doubling earnings per share again — a continued growth rate around 36% — which is not likely. So I do not think it is worth investigating further: on its historical trend it is overvalued, quite heavily so.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong (for the Sell case)

  • The AI-accelerator story runs hotter for longer: Q1 FY2026 AI semiconductor revenue was guided to roughly double year on year to $8.2bn. If custom silicon for the hyperscalers keeps compounding at that pace, my 20% growth assumption will look far too cautious — the single biggest risk to a Sell.
  • Explosive near-term growth is plausible: I said it myself — they might well see explosive growth over the next three years. A couple more years at 30%+ earnings growth would close a good part of the valuation gap.
  • The VMware software annuity: Subscriptions and services revenue of $19.0bn gives a sticky, recurring earnings base that deserves a premium multiple against ordinary chip cyclicality.
  • Formidable cash generation: $27.5bn of operating cash flow, cash up 73% to $16.2bn, and long-term debt cut by $4.3bn in a year — the balance sheet is strengthening rapidly.
  • Momentum: Mega-cap AI names have repeatedly stayed more expensive for longer than value frameworks suggest they should.

Bottom Line — Sell

Broadcom's numbers are monumental — revenue up 23.9% to $63.9bn, operating income up 89%, net income nearly quadrupled, and a balance sheet strengthening by the quarter. The growth record over four years is astounding, averaging roughly 33% a year. None of that is in dispute.

What is in dispute is the price. At $376.71 the market is extrapolating a volatile, high-thirties growth rate indefinitely — to justify it you would have to hope for something like doubling earnings per share again. On my deliberately more measured inputs the shares are worth around $120, a value opportunity of −68.1%. Even on the full historical growth rate they would still screen heavily overvalued. I will not be investigating further.
