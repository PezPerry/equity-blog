Bad Results, Real Assets

Interim results for the six months to 30 April 2026, published today (16 July 2026) after a delay from June to complete the covenant reset process with lenders. Crest Nicholson’s financial year ends 31 October. All figures in pounds sterling.

Briefing

  • Thesis: These are bad results — the market does not like them, the shares are volatile, and the company is operating under a covenant waiver while it renegotiates with its lenders. And yet the assets are worth considerably more than the market is paying.
  • The half: Revenue fell 20.8% to £197.6m, completions fell 21.0% to 584, adjusted gross margin collapsed 720 basis points to 7.0%, and net debt shot up to £141.8m from £71.5m. A statutory pre-tax loss of £35.2m and a loss per share of 10.1p. No interim dividend.
  • The overhang: The interest cover covenant would have been breached at 30 April. Lenders have granted a temporary waiver to 31 August. The equity is, right now, an option on that negotiation concluding successfully — and the probability strongly favours resolution.
  • Valuation view: I value this on assets, not earnings. Net asset value of £688.8m, discounted by a deliberately huge 40%, gives £413.28m — or 160.97p per share. Against a 67.00p share price, that is a +140.3% value opportunity.
  • Overall stance: Buy, research grade A+. Not because it is safe — but because the asymmetry between the asset value and the price is the widest on my current list.
Note on short interest: Short selling on Crest Nicholson sits at around 8.1% of the stock across multiple funds — a high level that weighs on the price. This is market data, not a position held by the author.

Business Model

Crest Nicholson is a UK housebuilder focused on Southern England, the Home Counties, the Midlands and, more recently, Yorkshire, positioning itself as a mid-premium builder. It buys land, secures planning permission, builds homes and sells them — across open market sales, affordable housing, and bulk deals to institutional buyers. It also periodically sells surplus land, which has historically been a meaningful profit contributor.

The key thing to understand for the valuation that follows is that a housebuilder is, in balance-sheet terms, a collection of land and half-built houses. That inventory — not the current year’s trading — is where the value sits, and it is why a company posting dreadful profits can still be worth considerably more than its share price implies.

Revenue fell 20.8% to £197.6m — a shockingly large drop. Completions fell 21.0% to 584, with the reduction spread across bulk deals (63 vs 107), affordable (107 vs 197) and open market (414 vs 435). The company says volumes slipped because of current economic uncertainty — but that is not what I am seeing from other housebuilders, which is why I have mapped their sites below. The open market sales rate was 0.48 against 0.53 a year earlier. Net debt shot up a shocking amount, to £141.8m from £71.5m — and £209.7m including land creditors.

They are saying gross margin slipped so dramatically because of the reduction in housing volume, which makes some sense. Of the £21.5m fall in adjusted gross profit: £12.7m was volume and mix, £3.5m lower land profit, £3.6m of additional net realisable value provisions from cutting prices on completed apartment stock, and £1.7m of completed-site costs. Cost inflation was about 3–4% on average, mostly on materials — which is fine, roughly in line with what we see at other companies.

🎓 Clarity What is a “net realisable value” (NRV) provision? Accounting rules say inventory must be carried on the balance sheet at the lower of what it cost to build and what it can actually be sold for. If a housebuilder finds it has to discount finished flats to shift them, and the discounted price is below the build cost, it must write the value down — that writedown is an NRV provision, and it hits profit immediately. Crest took a further £3.6m this half on completed apartment stock it is having to discount. It matters for two reasons: it tells you the company is cutting prices to sell, and it is a live risk to the asset value that underpins my valuation. Notably, Crest carries £1,052.5m of inventories after an NRV provision of just £17.1m — a small cushion against a big book.

The land disposal programme is still ongoing — one transaction completed in the half, with total land-sale revenue for the year expected around £40m and cash proceeds of around £50m contracted to settle in the second half (plus around £10m into FY27). No interim dividend is proposed.

🎓 Clarity Covenants, interest cover, and “material uncertainty” — what do they mean? When a company borrows, the lender attaches conditions called covenants — financial tests the borrower must keep passing. Break one, and the loan can become repayable on demand. The test in trouble here is the Interest Cover Ratio: essentially operating profit divided by interest paid, which asks the simple question “does this business earn enough to cover the interest on its debt?” With guided operating profit of £5–10m against £15m of interest, the answer is currently no. A waiver is the lender temporarily agreeing not to apply the test — which is what Crest has, until 31 August. “Material uncertainty related to going concern” is the formal accounting language auditors require when there is a real risk a company might not survive twelve months. It is a serious flag — but it is a statement about risk, not a prediction of failure.

The covenant problem

The Group has three covenants; the one in trouble is the Interest Cover Ratio — essentially EBIT divided by interest. With guided EBIT of £5–10m against £15m of interest, the ratio fails arithmetic that any lender would run. The company confirms the covenant would have been breached if measured at 30 April 2026. Lenders have granted a temporary waiver: the ratio will instead be measured at 31 August 2026 and reported by 30 September 2026, and during the waiver the credit facility is capped at the £175m already drawn. The company’s expectation is that a new covenant will replace the test before the waiver ends — discussions are described as well-advanced, positive and productive — but the replacement covenants, and Crest’s ability to comply with them, are not yet confirmed.

Base case vs Severe But Plausible

The base case assumes sales rates stay below long-term averages, land receipts in line with recent years, and stable outlets. Even in this base case, the existing covenant is not expected to be met once the waiver ends. If that happened and no amendment or extension were agreed, it would be an event of default: the facility and the loan notes would become repayable on demand, and the Group states plainly that it would not expect to have the liquidity to repay.

The Severe But Plausible downside then layers on: sales falling to around 1,400 units a year, a 5% cut to selling prices from October 2026, 3% build-cost inflation on non-fixed costs, land sale receipts halved to £15m a year, and a 21% (£4m) rise in interest costs. In that case the Group would additionally need to refinance the £50m notes before August 2027, because it could not repay them. Mitigations modelled include slowing build rates, cutting land buying and removing dividends. The directors believe the Group can continue for at least twelve months, but a material uncertainty exists that “may cast significant doubt on the Group’s ability to continue as a going concern.”

What it actually means

Crest Nicholson’s survival in its current form depends on its lenders agreeing new covenant terms in the next ten weeks. The equity is, right now, an option on that negotiation concluding successfully. If the amendment is signed — which both sides evidently expect, since the banks have already extended waivers twice and the alternative (forcing a fire-sale of a land bank into a weak market) serves nobody — the going-concern language falls away and attention returns to the operational recovery. If it were not signed, the debt becomes repayable on demand and the equity would be substantially at risk.

The probability strongly favours resolution: lenders extended rather than enforced, the security (land and homes) is real and liquid, and the disposal programme is bringing £50m of contracted cash in the second half. But it is the reason the shares trade at 67p against 268p of stated net asset value per share — and it is why my valuation applies a very large discount.

The Site Map — Why Have Their Sales Been Hit So Hard?

To understand why Crest’s volumes have fallen where other housebuilders’ have held up, I have mapped every live and pipeline development the company lists — 50 sites in all (42 live, 8 coming soon).

The map makes the problem visible. This is overwhelmingly a Southern and Eastern England footprint — Hampshire, Surrey, Berkshire, Sussex, Kent, Essex and the Home Counties — plus a Midlands band and a small, newer Yorkshire cluster. There is nothing in Scotland, Wales or the North West.

That geography carries the highest selling prices in the country: Crest’s open-market average selling price is £414k against a UK new-build average nearer £300k. At a 3.75% base rate — which markets now expect to stay higher for longer — it is exactly these higher-price, deposit-heavy Southern buyers whose affordability is most stretched, while the volume builders weighted to cheaper Northern and Midlands stock have reported firmer sales rates through the same period. Add to that a small estate — just 41 average outlets, so each slow site moves the needle — the closure of the Chiltern division in November 2025, and completed apartment stock that is having to be discounted. It is a concentrated, high-price, South-weighted book selling to the most rate-sensitive buyers in the country.

I would say though, that this is a theoretical reason. While it might make some sense, other southern and eastern based housebuilders do not appear to be having the same issues.

Net Asset Value

Also concerning me: net asset value has slipped from £726.8m to £688.8m over the past twelve months — a 5.2% downward shift — driven by the statutory loss and the increase in interest-bearing borrowings. That is not good.

But the composition of those assets matters enormously for the valuation: £1,052.5m of inventories, of which £632.1m is land, £311.6m work-in-progress and £83.9m completed buildings — carried after a net realisable value provision of just £17.1m. These are real, saleable assets.

What Matters for This Company

The margins have completely slipped, and I don’t fully understand how they have managed to fall so far on volumes where other property companies have held up — though the map above supplies some answer: a concentrated, high-price Southern estate selling to the most rate-sensitive buyers, at the moment consumer confidence wobbled.

The short term is likely to be quite choppy on a trading basis: these are bad results, the market does not like them, there is a lot of share-price volatility, and there is substantial short interest — 8.1% of the stock across multiple funds — which weighs on the price. The covenant negotiation is an overhang, and until it signs, the shares will trade on fear rather than fundamentals.

But here is the thing: they still hold land and units for sale that are truly worth something — quite a lot, in fact, because land with planning in Southern England is a relatively liquid kind of asset that will be of interest to potential buyers. Even taking in all of the issues — the covenant, the losses, the debt, the legacy provisions — I would claim the company is still very much worth a considerable amount of money, even though the operating performance is terrible.

Valuation

For my valuation I am continuing to use net asset value rather than earnings — earnings are at a cyclical trough and tell you nothing about what the assets would fetch. Net asset value stands at £688.8m. I am discounting it by 40% — a huge discount, deliberately so, to absorb the covenant risk, further writedowns, the legacy provisions and everything else on the worry list — which takes it down to £413.28m, or 160.97p per share. The growth rate and valuation multiple are ignored in asset mode. I apply a 20% risk factor and a 50% probability, and I am a buyer.

🎓 Clarity Why value a company on assets instead of earnings? Most of my valuations start with earnings per share — what the business earns, multiplied out. That approach breaks down when profits are at a cyclical low or negative, as Crest’s are: multiplying a trough loss tells you nothing useful. So instead I use net asset value (NAV) — everything the company owns minus everything it owes. For a housebuilder that is mostly land and part-built homes, which have a real market price regardless of this year’s profit. The catch is that book value is an accounting estimate, not a guaranteed sale price — land might fetch less in a forced sale, and provisions might be topped up. That is exactly why I apply a large discount (40% here) rather than taking the stated figure at face value.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • The covenant amendment is not signed: The single biggest risk. If lenders do not agree new terms before the waiver ends, the facility and loan notes become repayable on demand — and the company states it would not have the liquidity to repay. The equity would be substantially at risk.
  • Dilution: Even in a resolution, a rescue equity raise at a depressed price would badly dilute existing holders — a real possibility my 40% discount is intended to absorb, but which could go further.
  • Further writedowns: Inventories of £1,052.5m carry an NRV provision of only £17.1m. If prices are cut harder, that cushion is thin and the asset value that underpins the whole case erodes.
  • The volume problem is company-specific: Other Southern housebuilders are not seeing the same collapse. If the cause is execution rather than geography, the recovery is slower and less certain than the map story suggests.
  • Legacy provisions keep growing: £200.0m of provisions remain, and the completed-sites estimate has already been topped up twice. The exceptional line will not fall silent soon.
  • Trading gets worse before it gets better: Guidance implies interest consuming the entire operating result, net debt doubling, and volumes down a further 11–17%. There is no quick operational fix.

What Would Change My Mind

  • The covenant amendment signing: The single clearest catalyst. It would remove the going-concern language, shift attention back to fundamentals, and likely force a sharp short-covering rally given 8.1% of the stock is short.
  • Land disposals landing at or near book value: The £50m of contracted second-half cash settling as planned would validate the asset values I am relying on. If land sells well below book, my discount was not big enough.
  • Sales rates stabilising: Any sign the 0.48 open-market rate is bottoming would suggest the volume collapse is cyclical rather than structural.
  • A rate cut or improving affordability: Crest is the most rate-sensitive builder on my list given its £414k average selling price. Falling rates would help it more than most.
  • An approach for the company or its land: At a quarter of net asset value, with a liquid Southern land bank, the asset value could be crystallised by a bidder rather than by management.

Bottom Line — Buy

Let me be blunt: these are bad results. Revenue down a fifth, margins halved, a pre-tax loss, net debt doubled, the dividend gone, and a covenant the company admits it would have breached. The equity is currently an option on a lender negotiation concluding in the next ten weeks, and until it signs the shares will trade on fear rather than fundamentals.

But they still hold land and units that are truly worth something — and land with planning in Southern England is a relatively liquid asset. At 67p the market pays around a quarter of stated net asset value, pricing something close to a distressed outcome. Even after a deliberately punitive 40% discount, I get to 160.97p — a +140.3% value opportunity.

This is not a Grade A+ because it is safe. It is A+ because the asymmetry between the asset value and the price is the widest on my current list.
