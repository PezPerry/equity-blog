Briefing

Business Model

Travis Perkins is the UK's largest distributor of building materials to the building, construction, and home improvement markets. The company was founded in 1797 and is headquartered in Northampton. It operates through two main segments:

  • Merchanting (approximately 80% of group revenue): The core business, operating under the Travis Perkins, BSS, Keyline Civils Specialist, and CCF brands. Travis Perkins is a general builders' merchant offering heavy-side products complemented by light-side convenience, tool hire, managed services, and kitchen design (Benchmarx). BSS provides specialist plumbing and heating distribution. Keyline supports housebuilders and infrastructure contractors. CCF distributes insulation and interior building products.
  • Toolstation (approximately 20% of group revenue): A multi-channel supplier of tools and building supplies to trade and DIY customers. Toolstation UK has been growing modestly and is the bright spot in the group. Toolstation Benelux (Belgium and Netherlands) is under strategic review and an exit looks increasingly likely.
🎓 Clarity What is a builders' merchant? A builders' merchant is a wholesaler that supplies building materials — timber, bricks, cement, plumbing supplies, insulation, tools — primarily to trade professionals (builders, plumbers, electricians) rather than to the general public. Their revenue is directly linked to the level of construction activity in the economy. When housebuilding and renovation activity is strong, merchants thrive. When construction slows, they suffer.

Gross Margin — the Slow Bleed

Things aren't looking good. The gross margin has been slowly reducing every year. It currently sits at 26.1%, which is the lowest I have seen for at least the last six years. I doubt it will improve much given the continued lack of volume — and the company is actively rationalising areas, which also hits turnover.

The Fundamental Question

How on earth are they going to resolve this? Sales figures aren't coming through. The gross margin is falling. The Merchanting division — which is the vast majority of the business — is in structural decline. Toolstation UK is a bright spot but too small to move the needle. Toolstation Benelux is being reviewed for exit.

The new CEO, Gavin Slark (joined 1 January 2026), has sector experience, but the headwinds are formidable: construction activity is subdued, cost inflation from the Middle East conflict is pushing up energy and material costs, and the company is trying to simultaneously restructure and maintain market share.

The balance sheet is at least in reasonable shape — net cash before leases of £1 million at FY 2025, with a £136 million working capital inflow helping to offset the operational challenges. But a strong balance sheet doesn't fix a declining top line and eroding margins.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Construction market remains subdued: UK construction activity shows no sign of meaningful recovery. Housebuilding starts remain depressed, and infrastructure spending has not yet translated into merchant volumes.
  • Merchanting in structural decline: £670 million of revenue lost over three years. If the sector does not recover, further branch closures and redundancies are inevitable.
  • Gross margin compression: Six consecutive years of declining gross margins (28.5% → 26.1%). If this continues, profitability will erode further even if volumes stabilise.
  • Adjusted vs statutory gap: £222 million of adjusting items in FY 2025 alone. The statutory operating loss of £97 million is a more honest reflection of where the business is.
  • Toolstation Benelux: Under strategic review, likely to be exited. This will crystallise losses and remove what management once hoped would be a growth engine.
  • Cost inflation: The Middle East conflict is pushing up energy and material costs, squeezing already thin margins.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Construction activity recovery: A sustained recovery in UK housebuilding starts and renovation activity would directly benefit Merchanting volumes. Without this, the business continues to shrink.
  • Gross margin stabilisation above 27%: If the company can arrest the margin decline and return to above 27%, it would signal that pricing power is returning.
  • Share price below 350p: At lower prices, the value opportunity would narrow and the dividend yield would become more supportive. The current price offers no margin of safety.
  • Successful Toolstation Benelux exit: A clean disposal at a reasonable price would remove a drag and allow management to focus entirely on the UK operations.
  • New CEO strategy clarity: Gavin Slark has been in the role for four months. A clear strategic plan for Merchanting — beyond cost-cutting — would be encouraging.

Conclusion — Sell

I don't really see what Travis Perkins can do to turn this around in the near term. The market is against them, the margins are compressing, and even on a generous three-year forward view the stock looks significantly overpriced.

If the construction market doesn't recover meaningfully, these problems only get worse.
