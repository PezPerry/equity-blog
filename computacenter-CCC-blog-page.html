Selling More, Earning Less

Computacenter PLC — a large-scale technology reseller with an attached IT services arm, selling to large corporations and the public sector. All figures in pounds sterling.

Briefing

  • Thesis: Revenue jumped 32% to £9.19 billion in FY 2025 — but almost all of it came from low-margin hardware reselling on the back of the AI infrastructure build-out. Group gross margin fell to 12.4%, its lowest in over five years. They are selling far more, but of the least profitable thing.
  • The profit picture: Despite the revenue surge, post-tax profit fell from £172m to £157m, and reported earnings per share dropped for a second year running to 145.5p. Profitability as a share of turnover slipped to just 2.0%.
  • Valuation view: On a deliberately conservative basis — reported earnings per share of 145.5p, an 8x multiple, a generous 2% growth rate and a 10% reduction — I arrive at a valuation of 1,215.22p. Against a current price of 4,248p, that's a value opportunity of −71.4%.
  • The bull's exhibit A: A record £7.1 billion product order backlog (up 200% in constant currency) says the AI-hardware demand is real and sitting in the pipeline for 2026. The market is paying 24x earnings precisely to capitalise that story.
  • Overall stance: Sell. The balance sheet is strong, but I don't like where B2B AI-infrastructure reselling is heading — it's a squeezed area, and the ability to turn big sales into profit is slowly eroding.

Business Model

Computacenter is a technology and services provider, marketing primarily to large corporations and the public sector. At heart it is a large-scale technology reseller — it buys hardware and software from manufacturers and sells it on to big organisations — with an attached IT services arm (professional services and managed services) bolted on top. The reselling generates enormous volume at thin margins; the services side is smaller but more profitable.

Revenue and Profitability

Revenues have oscillated a bit over recent times but, in general, have not really been heading downwards. FY 2021 saw a negative period of −7.5%, which we can largely forgive given the lockdown impacts of the COVID pandemic. Things bounced back dramatically in FY 2022, with revenue growth of 28.5% to £6.471bn. FY 2023 saw sales rise 7.0% to £6.923bn, FY 2024 was nearly flat at +0.6% to £6.965bn, and then FY 2025 saw a sudden jump again — revenue up 32% to £9.194bn. Interestingly, I saw something similar recently with Softcat, which operates in a very similar space.

Their gross margins are reasonably poor — which is what I would anticipate in a sector like this — but they were especially low this year, hitting 12.4% in FY 2025, the lowest in over five years. Generally they have oscillated around 14–15%. Profitability has remained reasonably flat year on year, although FY 2025 did see post-tax profit reduce from £172m to £157m. That meant earnings per share went down for the second year in a row.

Source: Computacenter annual reports. The "selling more, earning less" story in one chart: revenue (navy) surged 32% to £9.19bn, yet gross margin (gold) fell to 12.4% — its lowest in over five years.

🎓 Clarity Why does a falling gross margin matter when sales are booming? Gross margin is the percentage of each pound of sales left over after the direct cost of the goods sold. For a reseller, it's structurally low — they buy hardware and sell it on for a relatively small mark-up. When Computacenter's revenue jumps a third but its gross margin falls to 12.4%, it means the extra sales are coming from the least profitable activity (bulk hardware) rather than the more profitable one (services). So the company is handling far more volume, and taking on more risk and working capital to do it, without the profit growing to match. Big top-line growth that comes with shrinking margins is exactly the kind of growth that flatters the headline but doesn't reach the bottom line.

🎓 Clarity Reported vs adjusted earnings per share — why two numbers? Reported (statutory) earnings per share is the official figure under accounting rules. Adjusted earnings per share strips out one-off items management considers non-recurring. This year the two diverge sharply: reported earnings per share fell 4.8% to 145.5p, dragged down by a £20.2m impairment (a one-off writedown of an asset's value), while adjusted earnings per share actually rose 9.5% to 175.1p. So the underlying trading was better than the headline "earnings down two years running" suggests. My valuation deliberately uses the lower, conservative reported figure of 145.5p — but it's only fair to flag that the adjusted read is more flattering.

Dividend and Balance Sheet

Dividends rose again last year despite profits slipping — baffling on the face of it, but in line with what a lot of companies seem to prioritise currently (dividend per share up to 74.6p). Even so, the yield is low at 1.8%, and given slipping profitability I would wonder whether they can really offer anything greater. Borrowing, though, is at a good level: net funds (negative net borrowing) of £426m positive. So the balance sheet is genuinely strong — that's the clear positive in this story.

Why Did Revenue Grow So Much?

This is an AI-infrastructure story, and specifically a North American one. The 32% surge was driven overwhelmingly by North America Technology Sourcing, where the company grew networking and data-centre volumes with both enterprise and hyperscale (cloud) customers on the back of buoyant demand for AI-related infrastructure. The UK contribution also leaned on AI data-centre projects. It is not primarily a government/public-sector upgrade cycle — if anything public-sector demand in Germany and France was soft for much of the year. It is the AI build-out, sold as hardware.

Two corroborating signals. First, the product order backlog ended 2025 at a record £7.1bn, up 200.3% year on year in constant currency, driven by Technology Sourcing order intake in North America and the UK — so the AI-hardware demand is sitting in the pipeline for 2026 too. Second, the company even ran a temporary North American facility holding £137.7m of inventory at year-end to support a single major data-centre programme.

What Matters for This Company

I don't think I'm going to investigate this company further. Their margins have always been a bit weak, but they seem especially low right now.

Since 11 February they've seen a 35% rise in share value, which I read as a market reaction to expectations of companies dramatically increasing infrastructure spend on AI requirements. But in any case, I don't really like where the company is heading. There is a lot not to like about business-to-business AI infrastructure: it is a squeezed area, and while this company can clearly achieve high total sales and do so profitably, its ability to make profit from those sales is slowly decreasing. I don't really see where this company — like other software and computer-service companies — is heading.

Valuation

I'm being a little generous here. I'm using the last reported earnings per share of 145.5p, a valuation multiple of 8x, and a growth rate of 2%. That is kinder than the company's recent record arguably deserves — earnings per share shrank 4.8% last year, and the two-year average is around −8.3% — but I suspect they will be able to achieve a 2% long-term growth rate, helped by the dramatic revenue rise. I then apply a 10% valuation reduction, because I don't like this whole infrastructure-reselling section: it's a squeezed area, and profitability is slowly eroding. I set a relatively low 15% risk factor, and — because I haven't researched the share heavily — a probability of 26%.

If the AI-hardware demand proves both large and lasting — the £7.1bn backlog is the bull's exhibit A — my 2% growth assumption will look far too cautious. Risks and What Could Go Wrong (for the Sell case)

  • The AI-hardware demand is large and lasting: The £7.1bn record backlog (+200% cc) is real and points to a strong 2026. If this is a multi-year build-out rather than a one-off, my 2% growth assumption is far too cautious — the single biggest risk to a Sell.
  • North America keeps compounding: US profit nearly doubled and now drives ~39% of group operating profit. Continued momentum there could carry earnings well past my estimate.
  • Strong balance sheet: £426m of net funds gives real resilience and optionality (buybacks, acquisitions, weathering a downturn).
  • Adjusted earnings tell a better story: On the adjusted measure, earnings per share rose 9.5% to 175.1p — the underlying trading is healthier than the reported figure I've used.
  • Mix could re-balance: If the higher-margin Services arm re-accelerates, group margins could recover from the 12.4% trough.

Bottom Line — Sell

Computacenter has just posted a blockbuster revenue figure — up 32% to £9.19bn — but it's the wrong kind of growth. It came almost entirely from low-margin hardware reselling on the AI build-out, group gross margin fell to a five-year low of 12.4%, and reported earnings per share dropped for a second straight year. They're selling far more, but of the least profitable thing.

The balance sheet is strong and the backlog is genuinely impressive — and if the AI-hardware demand proves durable, I'll have been too cautious. But on the quality of earnings as they stand, and on a deliberately conservative valuation, the shares look dramatically overvalued at −71.4%. I don't like where B2B infrastructure reselling is heading, and I won't be looking at this one further.

Sell. Value opportunity: −71.4%. Probability: 26%. Research grade: C.
