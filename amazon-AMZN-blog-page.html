Doing a Great Job, But Priced About Right

All figures in US dollars. Amazon reports in USD; the tear sheet shows earnings per share in cents (717 = $7.17).

Briefing

  • Thesis: Amazon is a maturing tech company consolidating into higher stability. Revenue growth has been steady rather than spectacular (FY 2025 up 12.4% to $716.9 billion), but gross margins have risen consistently to 50.3% and earnings per share has compounded at roughly 28% a year over five years. The profit engine is overwhelmingly Amazon Web Services (AWS).
  • Valuation view: Using earnings per share of $7.17, a 9x multiple, a 28.5% long-term growth rate, and an 8% valuation uplift, I arrive at a valuation of $248.45. Against a current price of $261.26, that's a value opportunity of −4.9% — basically fair value.
  • Key strength: AWS grew 28% to $37.6 billion in Q1 — its fastest rate in fifteen quarters — and is the most profitable division, benefiting directly from AI demand. The advertising business now runs at roughly $70 billion annually, growing 22%.
  • Key risk: Capital expenditure has exploded — from around $40 billion in FY 2020 to roughly $132 billion in FY 2025, with FY 2026 expected materially higher again. The returns on this AI and infrastructure spend are still to be proven.
  • Overall stance: Hold. Doing a great job, but priced about right. No buy here — though short-term share-price weakness could present a buying opportunity.

Business Model

Amazon operates across three reporting segments: North America and International (the retail, marketplace, subscription and advertising businesses) and Amazon Web Services (AWS) (global cloud computing — compute, storage, database and increasingly artificial intelligence services).

The crucial point for investors is that these segments have very different economics. The retail business generates the overwhelming majority of revenue but operates on thin margins. AWS generates a much smaller share of revenue but the overwhelming majority of profit. So while Amazon looks like a retailer by turnover, it behaves like a high-margin cloud-and-advertising company by profit.

Turnover and Profit History

In recent times, Amazon's turnover growth has been fairly lacklustre — 9.4% in FY 2022, 11.8% in FY 2023, 11.0% in FY 2024, and 12.4% in FY 2025. Not huge rises in revenue growth. But at the same time, they have seen gross margins rise quite consistently, hitting 50.3% in FY 2025. You can really see the potential here: Amazon is a fully maturing tech company that is managing to consolidate its position into higher levels of stability.

🎓 Clarity Why does the revenue-versus-profit split matter so much? Amazon's retail businesses (North America and International) bring in 82% of total revenue but only 43% of operating profit, because selling and shipping physical goods is a low-margin activity. AWS, by contrast, sells cloud computing — which carries far higher margins — so it produces 57% of all profit from just 18% of revenue. When AWS grows faster than retail (as it is now, at 20%+), group profit grows much faster than group revenue. This is exactly why Amazon's earnings per share has compounded at ~28% a year while revenue has grown at only ~12%.

AWS and Advertising — the Growth Stories

The AWS revenue in Q1 rose by 28% to $37.6 billion — its fastest growing rate in fifteen quarters. It remains Amazon's most profitable division, and seems to be benefiting directly from AI demand as enterprises build and run AI workloads on Amazon's infrastructure.

Interestingly, Amazon's advertising business generated $17.2 billion in Q1 and now runs annually at around $70 billion, with growth of approximately 22% year on year. Very impressive — and another high-margin revenue stream that flatters the group's profit profile.

Valuation

What I've done with my valuation of Amazon is take the current earnings per share of $7.17, a valuation multiple of 9x, and continue with their historic growth rate — I'm giving it a 28.5% earnings per share growth rate into the long term. That seems very high, but they're achieving improvements in earnings per share over quite an elongated period, and as a major tech company they seem to be at the forefront of AI movements.

In a slightly different way to Alphabet, Meta, or Nvidia, Amazon has its own offerings — they're not all fighting in exactly the same space. I'm quite impressed with their general growth figures, and I think improvements in AI are likely to help their bottom line — in AWS infrastructure, in potential new products, and even on the retail side through better automation and robotics. As a result, I'm giving it a positive uplift of 8%, as I think they're likely to come out with some bumper figures in the coming year.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Capex burden: Capital expenditure has gone from around $40 billion to roughly $132 billion in five years, with FY 2026 expected materially higher. If the AI returns don't materialise, this is an enormous drag on free cash flow.
  • Valuation: At ~36x trailing earnings, the stock prices in a lot of future growth. The 28.5% growth assumption is high — any slowdown would make the current price look expensive.
  • Retail growth stalling: The retail side is "not growing so much." With margins thin there, the group increasingly depends on AWS and advertising to drive profit.
  • AWS competition: Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud are formidable competitors. Any loss of AWS momentum or pricing power would hit the profit engine directly.
  • Share-price volatility: The stock saw a sudden ~13% drop in February at the onset of the Iranian conflict. These swings create risk — but also opportunity.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Short-term share-price weakness: Should the price fall for short-term reasons — as it did in February — it could present a clear buying opportunity for a longer-term trade.
  • AWS re-acceleration sustained: If AWS holds growth above 25%, the profit engine would justify a higher valuation and a buy rather than a hold.
  • Evidence of capex payoff: Clear demonstration that the AI infrastructure spend is generating incremental high-margin revenue would support the 28.5% growth assumption.
  • Retail margin improvement: Better automation and robotics lifting the thin retail margins would add a second profit lever alongside AWS.
  • Advertising momentum: Continued 20%+ growth in the ~$70 billion advertising business would further improve the group's profit mix.

Bottom Line — Hold

I think the company is doing a great job and is ultimately likely to extend things further into the long term. Quite how and what they're going to do, who knows — but they've got huge amounts of cash flow and funds to accelerate their own business, and they're generating good profitability year on year.

Importantly, Amazon isn't fighting in quite the same space as Apple, Google or Microsoft — and that's probably a positive, because it has its own distinct offerings. At −4.9%, the stock is basically fair value, so no buy at this point — but should the share price present a short-term buying opportunity, the shares would be worth looking into for a longer-term trade.

Hold. Value opportunity: −4.9%. Probability: 50%. Research grade: C.
