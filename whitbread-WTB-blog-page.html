Rich in Property, Short on Growth

Whitbread PLC — owner of Premier Inn — under activist pressure from Corvex Management. All figures in pounds sterling.

Briefing

  • Thesis: Whitbread, the FTSE 100 owner of Premier Inn, is under activist pressure from Corvex Management (a ~7% holder) demanding the company put itself up for sale. On a conventional earnings basis the shares look expensive, but the case rests on a very strong owned-property portfolio worth far more than the market cap.
  • Valuation view: Using reported (statutory) FY 2026 earnings per share of around 122p, an 8x multiple, a 4% growth rate and a 30% valuation reduction, I arrive at a valuation of 904.78p. Against a current price of 2,338p, that's a value opportunity of −61.3%.
  • Key risk: Earnings power is weak — reported earnings per share fell 12.1%, revenue is flat, and the company has, on my reckoning, lost its way on growth. Net borrowing has risen to £4.72 billion while book equity has fallen.
  • Key tension: My earnings-based valuation says heavily overvalued, yet the freehold estate (£4.84 billion of property) is worth far more than the market cap. Both can be true — and which matters depends on whether a sale process actually happens.
  • Overall stance: Sell. Research grade B. Turbulence ahead — but a successful sale could crystallise the asset value the bulls point to.
Note on short interest: Short selling on Whitbread sits at around 5.16% of the stock — a high level — reflecting widespread market scepticism. This is market data, not a position held by the author.

The Corvex Situation

I'm looking at the company on the back of the 18 May news report, where Corvex Management — who own a 7% stake — demanded that Whitbread put itself up for sale.

Corvex slammed the company's new five-year plan, claiming it doubled down on many of the core value-destructive components they opposed in the original plan. Corvex's managing partner Keith Meister called the status quo untenable, said the shares trade at an unacceptable discount to intrinsic value, and demanded the board immediately retain an independent investment bank and commit to a comprehensive sale process. He noted that at the time of writing the stock traded at a 13-year low, on less than 8x pre-tax profit — a fraction of Whitbread's UK freehold asset value alone. Corvex has threatened to nominate new directors to the board if the company refuses to act.

Sources: Reuters, Sharecast, Skift, Hedgeweek (18 May 2026). Corvex first disclosed a 6.05% stake in December 2025; it now holds around 7% (LSEG data showed 6.1%).

🎓 Clarity What is an activist investor, and what's "sale-and-leaseback"? An activist investor buys a large stake in a company specifically to push for changes they believe will unlock value — here, Corvex wants Whitbread to sell itself or its assets. "Sale-and-leaseback" is when a company sells a property it owns (a freehold) to raise cash, then leases it back so it can keep operating from it. It brings in money now, but the company no longer owns the building — so it loses the asset backing while taking on long-term rent commitments. That trade-off is central to the Whitbread debate: the freeholds are exactly what give the shares their floor.

The Five-Year Plan

The five-year plan targets, by FY 2031: £275 million of incremental adjusted pre-tax profit, net capital expenditure cut by £1 billion, and growth funded by £1.5 billion of freehold disposals (sale-and-leaseback). The room target is 96,000 rooms by FY 2031 against around 86,000 now — only a 10.8% increase — with a longer-term aspiration of 125,000 rooms. The plan also involves exiting the 197 remaining branded restaurants and cutting around 3,800 jobs, turning Whitbread into a "pure-play" hotel business and a majority leaseholder for the first time since Premier Inn was founded.

The Growth Problem

Looking at the FY 2026 figures, earnings per share was around 122.4p — down on FY 2023, FY 2024 and FY 2025. Revenue was flat in the last twelve months, and down 1.3% the year before. In the preceding three years they had okayish revenue growth (12.7% in FY 2024, 54.1% in FY 2023), but a lot of that simply reflects the recovery from much lower COVID-era figures.

What I find concerning: since 2018 — when they presumably no longer had Costa Coffee as part of the operation — they have, on average across the whole period, only managed to increase revenue by approximately 5.8% per year. Within that they had COVID, which significantly affected two years. But I think they have very much lost the way forward on how to grow the operation, despite what the new five-year plan might indicate.

It is quite difficult for them to expand within the UK market — I imagine it is reasonably expensive to do so — and I believe the German expansion is also not working out particularly well. It is very small in scale, both in the number of rooms in each region and the revenue per region.

But selling freeholds to fund growth erodes exactly the asset backing that gives the shares their floor.

Valuation

I'm using the reported FY 2026 earnings per share of around 122p with a valuation multiple of 8x. I'm not seeing much evidence of earnings-per-share growth, but I'll set a growth rate of 4%, with a 30% reduction to the valuation because I think the company has lost its way a little. Earnings per share is dropping, revenue is struggling, and they have to do significant capital expenditure to widen their range — capex that is coming at the cost of selling down the property portfolio. That isn't what I want to see. I understand why management might go down that route — after all, they're meant to be a hotel operator, not a property speculator — but I think it will put strain on the company.

Management have also lost a lot of credibility in my mind because of the adventures in Germany, and their lack of success there. The German venture itself would have been fine — it's management's continued assertions that it's going well, despite evidence to the contrary, that erode trust.

The Tension Worth Acknowledging

There's an honest tension at the heart of this one. My conventional earnings-based valuation says heavily overvalued (−61.3%), yet Corvex's entire thesis — and the property-backed argument I agree with — is that the freehold estate is worth far more than the market cap.

The two are not contradictory. The earnings power is weak relative to the share price, while the breakup/asset value is high. Which matters more depends on whether a sale process actually happens. My Sell reflects the earnings lens and execution risk; a successful sale could crystallise the asset value the bulls point to.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong (for the Sell case)

  • A sale actually happens: If Corvex succeeds and a sale or break-up process crystallises the £4.84 billion property value, the shares could re-rate sharply upward — the main risk to a Sell call.
  • Asset value as a floor: The freehold estate provides genuine downside protection that a pure earnings valuation ignores. The shares may not fall as far as the earnings lens implies.
  • Activist pressure forcing change: Corvex's board-nomination threat could force management into value-accretive action sooner than expected.
  • Dividend support: The 4.2% yield and continued buybacks (£419 million returned in the year) provide some support for the share price.
  • Germany turning: Premier Inn Germany reached its first annual profit — if it scales better than I expect, the growth picture improves.

My View

My proposed action is a Sell, with a research grade of B and a value opportunity of −61.3%. There is some turbulence ahead for this company.

Looking at the share price, they are down 30% over the last three years and 19% over the last five months. Note too that, although the shares have fallen dramatically, short selling on the stock sits at around 5.16%, which is high. They have issues coming up and more headwinds, both macroeconomic and operational, with a lot of questions to answer.

Bottom Line — Sell

On the conventional, cash-real earnings lens I prefer, Whitbread looks heavily overvalued at −61.3%. Earnings per share is falling, revenue has stalled, debt is rising while book equity falls, and I think management have lost their way on growth — and some credibility over Germany.

The honest counter-argument is the property: £4.84 billion of freeholds that are worth far more than the earnings suggest. If Corvex forces a sale, that value could be crystallised. But that's a bet on a process, not on the business — and on the business, the shares are too expensive.

Sell. Value opportunity: −61.3%. Probability: 60%. Research grade: B.
