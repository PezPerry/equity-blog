Cheap on Paper, But Show Me the Like-for-Like Growth

Briefing

  • Thesis: JD Sports released FY 2026 results showing solid revenue growth of 10.5% to £12.7 billion, driven largely by the full-year contributions from Hibbett and Courir. But organic revenue growth was a more modest 2.1%, like-for-like sales declined 2.1%, and adjusted earnings per share fell for the third consecutive year to 11.71p. The company has relied on acquisitions to grow while organic profitability per share has been eroding.
  • Valuation view: Using reported earnings per share of 8.50p with a conservative 3% growth rate and no valuation discount, I arrive at a valuation of approximately 84.3p. Against a current price of 68.5p, the value opportunity is +23.2%.
  • Key risk: Like-for-like sales have been negative, margins are compressing, and FY 2027 guidance points to a further profit decline (profit before tax guided at £750–850 million versus £852 million in FY 2026).
  • Key catalyst: Free cash flow improved 36% to £462 million and the balance sheet is healthier with net cash of £311 million. The £100 million share buyback programme at current depressed prices is meaningful.
  • Overall stance: Hold. At 68.5p with a trailing price-to-earnings ratio of 5.9x, the stock is optically cheap — but I want to see like-for-like improvement before committing.

Business Model

JD Sports Fashion is one of the world's largest omnichannel retailers of sports fashion brands. Founded in Bury in 1981, the company operates approximately 4,870 stores globally across multiple fascias. The core brand is JD, complemented by a growing portfolio of "Complementary Concepts" — regional athletic retailers acquired to extend geographic reach.

The business model centres on curating and retailing premium sports fashion footwear and apparel from brands including Nike, Adidas, Jordan, New Balance, and others. JD differentiates through exclusive product allocations from key brand partners, particularly Nike and Jordan, which rely on JD's ability to connect with younger, fashion-conscious consumers.

Geographically, the group has shifted significantly through acquisitions. North America now accounts for approximately 40% of group revenue, up from around 32% two years ago, primarily driven by the Hibbett and DTLR/Shoe Palace acquisitions. Europe (including the UK) accounts for approximately 50%, with Asia Pacific and the rest of the world making up the remainder.

Sales are up 11.7% on a constant currency basis which is good. But the growth is largely a result of the full-year contribution from Hibbett (acquired July 2024) and Courir (acquired November 2024). Organic sales growth was a more modest +2.1%, and critically, like-for-like sales declined 2.1%. That underlying softness is a concern.

🎓 Clarity What is the difference between revenue growth and like-for-like growth? Revenue growth includes the contribution from newly acquired businesses and new store openings. Like-for-like growth (also called "comparable" or "same-store" sales) only measures stores that have been open for at least a year. A company can report strong revenue growth driven by acquisitions while its existing business is actually shrinking. That is exactly what is happening at JD Sports — headline revenue up 10.5%, but the underlying like-for-like figure is −2.1%.

The Earnings Per Share Problem

The earnings per share trajectory is worth flagging. Despite revenue nearly doubling since FY 2022 (from £8.6 billion to £12.7 billion), adjusted earnings per share has actually declined from 12.8p to 11.7p. The adjusted figure has now declined for three consecutive years.

The company has relied on inorganic growth to fuel revenue increases, while organic profitability per share has been eroding. This is the central tension in the investment case.

The Acquisition Machine — Five Years of Dealmaking

JD Sports has been one of the most acquisitive retailers in the world over the past five years, spending approximately £1.9 billion on five major acquisitions. The strategy has been to build a global multi-brand platform, particularly expanding into the US and European markets through "Complementary Concepts" — regional athletic retailers that operate alongside the core JD fascia.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Like-for-like sales decline persisting: LFL sales were −2.1% in FY 2026 and −2.3% in Q1 FY 2027. If this doesn't improve, no amount of acquisitions will fix the underlying business.
  • Margin compression: Operating margin has fallen from 8.2% to 7.0% in a single year. FY 2027 guidance suggests further decline.
  • Acquisition integration risk: Approximately £1.9 billion spent on acquisitions in four years is a lot of integration to manage simultaneously. If Hibbett or Courir underperform, the capital deployed will not generate adequate returns.
  • Nike product cycle dependency: JD relies heavily on Nike and Jordan for exclusive allocations. Expiring product cycles at brand partners are already pressuring footwear demand.
  • Consumer sentiment: The Iranian crisis, higher inflation, and uncertain interest rate direction are all weighing on discretionary retail spending.
  • Adjusted versus reported earnings gap: Adjusted earnings per share of 11.71p versus reported 8.63p — a meaningful gap driven by acquisition-related charges that are likely to persist.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Like-for-like sales returning to positive: This is the single most important metric. If LFL turns positive for two consecutive quarters, it would signal genuine organic momentum.
  • Operating margin stabilising above 8%: A return to FY 2025 margin levels would demonstrate that the cost base is under control despite the acquisitions.
  • Share price below 55p: At lower prices, the value opportunity widens significantly and the buyback programme becomes even more accretive.
  • Hibbett integration delivering $25 million synergies on schedule: Proof that the US acquisitions are generating the promised cost savings would validate the strategy.
  • Nike product cycle refresh: A strong new product cycle from Nike and Jordan would directly drive footfall and demand at JD stores globally.

Conclusion — Hold

The share price decline reflects real operational challenges: declining like-for-like sales, compressing margins, and a cautious outlook for FY 2027.

At 68.5p, with a price-to-earnings ratio of 5.9x on reported earnings, the stock is cheap on an absolute basis. The 3% growth rate in my model is deliberately conservative — if the business returns to historical growth rates, the upside would be significantly greater.

But I want to see the like-for-like improvement before committing. Show me the organic growth.

Value opportunity: +23.2%. Probability: 50%.
