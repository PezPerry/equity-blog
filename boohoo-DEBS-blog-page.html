Business Model

Boohoo Group (now transitioning to Debenhams Group) is a UK-based online fashion retailer. The company operates five core brands: boohoo, boohooMAN, PrettyLittleThing (collectively the "Youth Brands"), Karen Millen (premium fashion), and Debenhams (online department store and marketplace).

The strategic direction is a shift from a traditional own-stock retail model towards a "stock-lite, capital-lite" marketplace model, led by the Debenhams brand. In a marketplace model, the company doesn't buy and hold inventory — third-party sellers list their products on the Debenhams platform, and the company takes a commission on each sale. This reduces the capital tied up in stock and warehousing, and improves cash flow.

The company is headquartered in Manchester, with a fully automated warehouse in Sheffield and a small London office. It recently completed a warehouse consolidation (closing the Daventry facility) and a technology re-platform.

What Matters for This Company

  • Adjusted earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation: This is management's preferred profitability metric. It came in at £53 million for FY 2026, up 36% year-on-year. However, this figure excludes share-based payments, exceptional items, and amortisation. The gap between this and statutory profit remains very wide — in FY 2025, adjusted earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation was £41.6 million while the statutory loss after tax was £263.3 million.
  • Gross merchandise value trend: Gross merchandise value (the total value of goods sold through the platform, before returns) has been declining but improving. The company exited February 2026 at only 5% below the prior year — three consecutive quarters of improvement. Getting back to growth on this metric is critical.
  • Marketplace growth: The Debenhams marketplace is the strategic centrepiece. Gross merchandise value on the marketplace grew 34% in FY 2025 and 20% in the first half of FY 2026. This is by far the most encouraging part of the business.
  • Fixed cost base: Management is targeting a fixed cost base of £100 million in FY 2027, down from £175 million in FY 2026. The exit rate as at year-end was already £119 million — £11 million better than the guided £130 million.
  • Net debt: Net debt was £90 million at the end of February 2026, less than 2 times adjusted earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation. The company expects this to fall below 1 times by end of FY 2027. The £40 million equity raise in February 2026 helped, but was dilutive to existing shareholders.
  • Cash flow improvement: Capital expenditure is expected to fall from £16 million to approximately £8 million. Lease costs are expected to drop from £18 million to approximately £13 million (and further to approximately £6 million once the vacant US property is exited). Depreciation will fall from approximately £59 million to approximately £20 million.
🎓 Clarity Why is the gap between adjusted earnings and statutory profit so important? Adjusted earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation strips out many real costs — depreciation (the wearing out of assets), amortisation (the gradual write-off of intangible assets), exceptional items (one-off charges), and share-based payments (compensation given to employees as shares). A company that reports healthy adjusted numbers but massive statutory losses year after year is effectively saying "our underlying business is fine, it's just all these other costs that are the problem." But those costs are real, and they reduce the cash available to shareholders. Until the statutory figures improve, the adjusted numbers should be treated with caution.

Recent Performance and Financials

The headline is that adjusted earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation came in at £53 million for FY 2026, approximately 30% ahead of original guidance and 36% higher year-on-year. I don't take the adjusted figure at face value yet — it's too open to potential manipulation, and I want to see the full results before making a judgement. That said, I'd accept that management are unlikely to be fully overstating the positivity. They appear to be making some genuine progress on the restructuring.

My Longer-Term View

In FY 2024 and FY 2025, they have essentially dumped a lot of costs to try and reset the company's operational base for the longer term. I think they are slowly getting there, but there is still a lot of work to do.

My expectation is that they can ultimately achieve revenue of approximately £750 million per annum at a profit before tax margin of around 5%, giving a profit before tax of roughly £37.5 million and a post-tax profit of approximately £28.1 million. I'd give it a 4% growth rate from that point, but I need to discount the valuation by about 20% because they are not there yet.

What I Don't Like

The operation they end up with, even after the restructuring, is not one I love. They face a fundamentally more competitive marketplace than the one they once had, particularly from foreign fast-fashion imports (Shein, Temu). Things they spent years developing — like automated warehousing at Daventry — they ultimately abandoned. Six years of sales growth has been effectively undone.

The company needs to demonstrate that it is operationally more nimble and more desirable than competing brands, and I don't really think they are managing to show that yet.

The Debenhams marketplace model is the bright spot (gross merchandise value up 34% in FY 2025, up 20% in the first half of FY 2026), but the Youth Brands (boohoo, boohooMAN, PrettyLittleThing) have been in steep decline and are being "right-sized" — which is a polite way of saying they are shrinking.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Adjusted metrics masking reality: The company has reported improving adjusted earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation for multiple years while simultaneously posting massive statutory losses. Until the statutory figures improve materially, there is a risk that the adjusted numbers are painting a misleadingly positive picture.
  • Competition from Shein and Temu: Foreign fast-fashion platforms are aggressively competing in exactly the market segments where boohoo's Youth Brands operate. This competition is structural, not cyclical.
  • Youth Brands in decline: boohoo, boohooMAN, and PrettyLittleThing are being "right-sized" — they are shrinking. If the decline continues beyond management's expectations, revenue could fall further.
  • Dilution: The £40 million equity raise in February 2026 was dilutive to existing shareholders. Further fundraising cannot be ruled out if the turnaround takes longer than planned.
  • Execution risk on the marketplace transition: Pivoting from own-stock retail to a marketplace model is a fundamental business model change. If marketplace growth stalls or take rates come under pressure, the strategy unravels.
  • Net debt still material: At £90 million, net debt is meaningful for a company of this size. Interest costs of £21 million in FY 2026 are a significant drag on profitability.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Statutory profit: If the company reports a statutory profit (not just adjusted), it would be a genuine inflection point and would prompt a re-evaluation.
  • Gross merchandise value returning to growth: Currently exiting February at −5% year-on-year. A return to positive gross merchandise value growth would signal the turnaround is translating into top-line recovery.
  • Share price below 14p: At lower prices, the value opportunity would widen to a level where the risk/reward becomes more attractive despite the execution uncertainty.
  • Marketplace gross merchandise value exceeding 50% of total: If the Debenhams marketplace becomes the dominant revenue stream, it would validate the asset-lite pivot and support higher margins.
  • Net debt below £50 million: A meaningful deleveraging would reduce financial risk and interest costs, freeing up more profit for shareholders.

Conclusion — No Action

I come up with a valuation of approximately 16.0p, which gives a negative value opportunity of −7.7%. The stock is still a little overvalued given the execution risk that remains.

But they are slowly getting closer — we are starting to get within the region of approaching fair value for the company. They're just not quite there yet.
