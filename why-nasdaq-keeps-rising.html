Why does the Nasdaq keep rising?

The headline index is up over 20% this year while the world frets about inflation, oil and the Iranian crisis. The answer isn't that "tech is up" — it's that the Nasdaq has quietly stopped behaving like a broad market index at all.

I've been doing a lot of valuation work recently on the giants — Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet — and the thing that strikes me is: while they carry colossal valuations, these are based on their most recent profit figures/potential for the future! I am finding them to be roughly speaking “fairly valued”.

That is not what I expected to find.

These companies are progressing at significant pace, and the earnings are doing a lot of the heavy lifting that sentiment usually gets blamed for.

So why is the Nasdaq still rising? It is because it is no longer really moving like a broad market index. It is moving like a concentrated basket of global, cash-generative, AI-exposed mega-cap companies!

The index is a handful of companies

The Nasdaq-100 includes the 100 largest non-financial companies listed on Nasdaq. But its actual behaviour is dominated by a tiny number of constituents. As of the 2 June 2026 close, the top 15 names accounted for roughly 74.8% of the index's value, with the top 10 alone at about 66%. So the central point is: what happens to the biggest 15 companies is, in practice, what happens to the Nasdaq-100.

It's not just the four horsemen

The rally is not just Apple, Microsoft, Amazon and Alphabet. A great deal of 2026's movement has come from the AI-infrastructure and semiconductor names sitting right alongside them. Looking at the last six months of market-cap moves across the top 15:

Look at the bottom of that table. Micron up 355%, Arm up 189%, AMD up 140% — the memory and AI-silicon complex has been the real driving force, not the household names at the top.

Interestingly, three of the four giants I value most closely (Microsoft, Meta, Tesla) have actually gone backwards over six months. The index is rising despite some of its most famous members, because the semiconductor and AI-infrastructure suppliers are carrying it.

Reuters has noted that AI-related investment has lifted profit estimates across semiconductors and the wider technology complex, and that the recent rally has been led by exactly these AI-exposed businesses. This isn't pure sentiment. Many of these companies are still producing substantial profits, free cash flow and revenue growth. Their valuations are colossal in absolute terms, but — as my own valuation work keeps showing — they generally don't look irrational.

Why inflation and Iran haven't bitten — yet

Energy disruption and inflation risk can hurt markets generally — but the direct earnings impact is not equal across the economy.

Airlines (easyJet, IAG, Jet2), housebuilders (Persimmon, Taylor Wimpey, Crest Nicholson), banks, retailers, industrial cyclicals — these are the businesses exposed to fuel costs, consumer confidence, funding costs and real-economy demand. I've watched the UK housebuilders take a 30+% hit over three months on precisely these fears.

The Nasdaq leaders are a different animal. They are typically more global, more asset-light — though I'd caveat that AI infrastructure is becoming capital-intensive — and they have far stronger pricing power. The market is judging that Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet and the AI/semiconductor companies are simply not where the economic pain shows up first. So far, it's been right: the tech rally has continued despite higher energy prices and Iran-related inflation worries.

The thesis — and the risk baked inside it

The Nasdaq keeps rising because the market believes the largest Nasdaq companies can keep compounding earnings faster than the macro headwinds can damage them. Inflation, oil and geopolitical risk all matter, but investors are currently judging that the giants can absorb those shocks better than the average company. And on recent numbers, that judgement isn't wrong — the earnings really are there.

But the very thing that explains why the Nasdaq keeps rising also explains how it could fall sharply. Concentration cuts both ways. Reuters put it bluntly: if the small number of tech stocks leading the market roll over, the indices themselves roll over. The whole edifice rests on the durability of those earnings, the returns on enormous AI capex, the weight of that capital-expenditure burden, and the valuation assumptions investors are prepared to extend to a dozen-odd names.

The Nasdaq is rising because its giants are rising — and because, for now, investors believe those giants can absorb inflation, oil shocks and geopolitical disruption better than the average company.

Strip those companies out and the rest of the Nasdaq looks far less spectacular: up perhaps 10% rather than 21%. The headline number is a story about fifteen companies. That's not a reason to be bearish — I remain comfortable that the megacaps are roughly fairly valued on current earnings. But it is a reason to know exactly what you own when you buy "the Nasdaq." You are not buying a diversified slice of American technology. You are buying a concentrated bet on a handful of extraordinary businesses continuing to be extraordinary.
