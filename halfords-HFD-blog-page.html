Business Model

Halfords is the UK's leading retailer of motoring, cycling, and car servicing products and services. The company operates through two main segments:

  • Retail (approximately 75% of revenue): Comprising Motoring (car parts, batteries, dashcams, in-car technology, child seats) and Cycling (bikes, e-bikes, accessories, repair). Halfords operates approximately 400 retail stores across the UK and Ireland, plus a significant online presence.
  • Autocentres (approximately 25% of revenue): A network of approximately 370 car servicing and repair garages, offering MOTs, tyres, servicing, and repairs. This division has been the more challenging part of the business, with labour cost pressures and recruitment difficulties for skilled technicians.

The company's strategy is to become a consumer and business motoring services platform, moving from pure product retail towards higher-margin services. The dividend yield of approximately 6.6% is notably high for a UK retailer.

🎓 Clarity What does "like-for-like" sales growth mean? Like-for-like (or "LFL") sales growth measures revenue change only from stores that have been open for at least a year. It strips out the effect of new store openings and closures, so it tells you whether the existing business is genuinely growing or shrinking. A 4.8% group LFL figure means that on a comparable basis, Halfords' existing stores sold 4.8% more than the same period last year — a genuine improvement in trading.

The Autocentres Question

A big question remains on the Autocentres side: have they managed to resolve the issue of labour costs squeezing margins? The difficulty in recruiting and retaining skilled technicians at affordable rates has been a persistent drag on Autocentres profitability. The 5.8% like-for-like growth is encouraging, but I want to see this translate into sustainable margin improvement, not just top-line growth.

Valuation

Given all of the above, I'm using my profit before tax assumption of £43 million, implying profit after tax of approximately £30.2 million and earnings per share of approximately 13.9p. I've revised my growth rate upwards to 5% — from the 3% I initially considered — reflecting the stronger trading, the company's own confidence in FY 2027, and the hedging protections in place.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Execution track record: Halfords have repeatedly disappointed on the execution of their general expansion strategy. One strong quarter doesn't erase that history.
  • Autocentres labour costs: Recruiting and retaining skilled technicians remains expensive. If labour costs rise faster than revenue, margin improvement will stall.
  • Consumer confidence: The Iranian crisis, potential for higher interest rates, and broader macro uncertainty could hit discretionary spending — particularly on cycling and car accessories.
  • Cycling volatility: Cycling sales have been highly volatile in recent years (pandemic boom followed by bust). The 6.4% like-for-like growth is welcome, but the category is unpredictable.
  • Hedging protection is temporary: The strong FY 2027 hedging position protects near-term costs, but beyond that, the company is exposed to whatever happens to energy, freight, and foreign exchange markets.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Two consecutive quarters of 4%+ like-for-like growth: One quarter could be a blip. Sustained growth would demonstrate genuine momentum and warrant upgrading the growth rate.
  • Autocentres margin expansion: Evidence that the 5.8% top-line growth is translating into higher margins — not just higher revenue — would be the single most important signal.
  • Share price below 110p: At lower prices, the 6.6% dividend yield becomes even more compelling and the value opportunity would turn positive.
  • FY 2027 profit before tax above £45 million: If the company delivers at the upper end of FY 2027 consensus, it would justify re-rating the growth rate higher and potentially moving to a Buy.
  • Successful services transition: Clear evidence that the shift from product retail to motoring services is driving higher-margin, recurring revenue.

Conclusion — Hold

I am pleasantly surprised with these results, but I would still like to see more over the coming months to be sure this isn't a one-off blip.

Halfords have repeatedly disappointed on the execution of their general expansion strategy, and I really want to see better evidence of it all working well before committing.

The macroeconomic situation — the Iranian crisis, potential for higher interest rates, and consumer confidence — also warrants caution. But the hedging position for FY 2027 provides a degree of protection that many peers lack.

Things are looking much better for the company. Hold.
