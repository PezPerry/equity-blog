Strong Results, with a Takeover in the Price

Watches of Switzerland Group PLC — the UK's largest luxury watch retailer, operating across the UK and US. All figures in pounds sterling. FY26 is a 53-week year to 3 May 2026, against a 52-week FY25 — the extra week flatters the headline comparisons and is adjusted for below. Results published 14 July 2026.

Briefing

  • Thesis: These are strong results, particularly on US growth, cash generation and balance-sheet repair — and better than the market expected before May's trading update. All in all, some pretty impressive figures; I am very happy with where the company is sitting.
  • Three essential adjustments: The 53rd week materially inflated headline revenue and second-half growth; core adjusted EBIT increased only 3.4% despite reported revenue increasing 10.7%, because margins contracted; and roughly 85% of the £57.6m increase in statutory profit before tax came from exceptional charges falling away.
  • The US story: The US now produces more revenue than the UK — £927.2m, 51% of Group revenue — and 62% of Group adjusted EBIT at a 10.4% margin versus 7.3% in the UK and Europe.
  • Valuation view: On the conservative statutory earnings-per-share base of 42.6p, 10% growth, an 8x multiple and an 8% uplift, I arrive at 662.52p against a current price of 749.50p — a value opportunity of −11.6%, with the gap largely explained by takeover optionality.
  • Overall stance: Hold. Operationally strong and cash-generative — but FY27 needs to deliver the promised margin recovery for earnings growth to catch up with revenue growth.

Business Model

Watches of Switzerland Group (ticker WOSG) is the UK's largest luxury watch retailer, operating across the UK and US through Watches of Switzerland, Goldsmiths, Mappin & Webb, Mayors, Betteridge, Deutsch & Deutsch, Analog:Shift and Hodinkee, with a complementary luxury jewellery offering and exclusive US distribution rights for Roberto Coin. It is the UK's largest retailer for Rolex, OMEGA, Cartier, TAG Heuer and Breitling.

Over FY24–FY26, revenue compounded at approximately 9% annually but adjusted EBIT (earnings before interest and tax) grew at only about 7%. Basic earnings per share rose 87%, while net debt reduced to £57m from £96m the year before — very impressive. This was also a beat against the company-published consensus (adjusted earnings per share 45.2p vs 41.7p expected), though much of it was disclosed in May: the £154.8m of adjusted EBIT is confirmation at the very top of the £152–155m guided range rather than a new surprise.

The 53rd-Week Distortion

The reported comparison is 53 weeks against 52. Management discloses headline revenue growth of 11% reported and 13% constant currency, but excluding week 53 it is 8% reported and 11% constant currency — the extra week contributed approximately £44m of revenue. That changes the second-half interpretation considerably: the apparent acceleration from 8% growth in H1 to 14% in H2 was largely calendar-driven. There was underlying constant-currency improvement, but it was considerably more modest than the H2 headline suggests. For FY27 comparisons, the relevant estimated FY26 52-week reported base is approximately £1.78bn rather than £1.83bn.

🎓 Clarity What is a 53-week year? Many retailers run their accounts on a weekly calendar — always ending the year on the same weekday — which means most financial years contain 52 weeks, but roughly every six years an extra week has to be added to realign with the calendar. That 53rd week is a full extra week of sales in the comparison, so growth rates against a 52-week prior year are mechanically flattered. Here it added around £44m of revenue and turned an underlying 8% reported growth rate into a headline 11%. Constant currency ("cc"), meanwhile, strips out the effect of exchange-rate moves — useful here because the group now earns over half its revenue in US dollars.

Unsurprisingly, the US was the main driver of growth, with year-on-year revenue growth of 18% (24% constant currency) against only 5% in the UK. Worth noting that the US now achieves higher turnover than the UK market — £927.2m in the year, 51% of Group revenue, up from 48% — and they clearly have a foothold there that they can expand upon: 65 showrooms and counting, and 62% of Group adjusted EBIT (£96.1m, up 13%) at a 10.4% margin versus 7.3% in the UK and Europe. The US produced roughly 80% of the Group's £176.4m revenue increase, and only about 12% of the US increase came from the newly acquired Deutsch & Deutsch business (four Rolex-anchored showrooms in Texas) — excluding it, US reported growth was still around 16%, so the growth is underlying.

The one qualification is currency: the average rate moved from around $1.28 to $1.34, an approximate £47m translation headwind. UK and Europe revenue rose 4% — or 7% adjusting for the 21 non-core showroom closures and the divestment of the final two European sites — but UK profitability weakened: adjusted EBIT fell −6% to £65.9m at a 7.3% margin (−80bp).

The vast majority of the business — 82.4% of revenue — is luxury watches; luxury jewellery and the rest are much smaller. The growth is encouraging: pre-owned watch revenue rose 22% (now over 8% of luxury watch revenue, implying more than £120m of sales), ecommerce grew 19% reported, retail luxury jewellery grew 17% (UK +12%, US +26%), and Roberto Coin wholesale grew 16%. Management says luxury jewellery, pre-owned and ecommerce collectively represent 24% of revenue and expects them to outgrow the Group — but diversification remains a problem: watches still produced around 80% of the absolute revenue increase.

Profit Quality and Margin Pressure

Adjusted EBIT rose +3.4%, margin from 9.1% to 8.5% (−60bp). Only £5.1m of incremental adjusted EBIT was generated from £176.4m of additional revenue — an incremental margin of approximately 2.9%. The decline reflected lower brand margin rates following US tariffs, gold-price-driven brand price and margin changes, product mix, US ecommerce infrastructure, higher Group marketing, and a one-off Roberto Coin department-store debtor write-down. Overheads grew 21.5% — much faster than revenue — on acquisitions, marketing and infrastructure investment. The Group's net margin fell −70bp to 35.6%, equivalent to approximately £12.8m of profit foregone versus holding the FY25 margin.

🎓 Clarity Statutory vs adjusted profit — and why an 87% EPS jump isn't quite what it seems. Statutory (basic) figures are the official accounting numbers; adjusted figures strip out items management deems one-off, such as impairments (writedowns of an asset's value — here, showrooms whose expected returns fell). In FY25 the statutory numbers were crushed by £57.7m of exceptional charges; in FY26 those shrank to £8.8m. That normalisation — not underlying trading — explains roughly 85% of the £57.6m rise in statutory profit before tax, and most of the 87% jump in basic earnings per share. The underlying earnings engine grew more modestly: adjusted earnings per share rose 8.7%. My valuation still uses the statutory 42.6p — the exceptionals have now largely washed through, and it is the more conservative base.

Cash Flow, Balance Sheet and Capital

Free cash flow rose to £161.7m (from £97.8m), with conversion jumping from 51% to 80% — though approximately 75% of the improvement came from the working-capital outflow shrinking from £52.2m to £4.3m, so £161.7m should not be treated as the recurring rate (management's FY27 guidance of around 70% conversion implicitly anticipates normalisation). Inventory discipline was good: stock rose only 2.4% against 11% revenue growth, fell from 27.1% to 25.1% of revenue, and management says underlying inventory declined. Cash was deployed into £65.9m of expansionary capital expenditure, £39.3m of acquisitions, a £13.8m buyback and £71.7m of debt repayment — taking net debt from £96.2m to £57.0m (0.28x EBITDA pre-IFRS 16).

Shares in issue come up at 231.41m on this update. That is around 1.35% more concentrated than two years ago — not very much, but the company has been pursuing growth in the United States and reducing net debt, so they have presumably had better uses for the money. Which is positive. The showroom estate tells the strategy: UK and Europe down 22 sites to 126, US up 5 to 65 — a deliberate reallocation from lower-productivity UK sites toward the US network, and the UK's 7% closure-adjusted growth suggests the rationalisation is improving estate productivity.

Capital expenditure of £60–70m for the coming year is down a little as a percentage of revenue, which is good — I don't want to see them escalating their capital expenditure much further. The guidance arithmetic matters: applied to the 52-week base, the margin range implies adjusted EBIT of roughly £167–183m and adjusted earnings per share of around 49–54p — potentially materially faster profit than revenue growth. The key dependency is the margin recovery; without it, the earnings case becomes considerably less attractive. Management says the first ten weeks of FY27 have been encouraging, but no numerical trading rate was disclosed.

The Takeover Backdrop

The shares rose sharply on 13 July after Reuters reported the company had held preliminary talks in recent months over potential offers to take it private — the chief executive reportedly engaging because he believes the market undervalues the business, with sources suggesting a price expectation well above 750p. No formal offer has been made and the company declined to comment. So the current 749.50p contains some takeover optionality that is separate from the underlying results. Also worth noting: the previous £3bn FY28 revenue ambition is absent from the new statement — management now presents directional mid-term priorities without a dated target, which reduces the risk of pursuing uneconomic growth but removes a degree of long-range visibility.

What Matters for This Company

All in all, some pretty impressive figures, and I am very happy with where the company is sitting. In FY26 they achieved a slight uptick in return on turnover at around 7.9% pre-tax profit on revenue. They have done a very good job of generating profit and moving around the issues at a more macro, global level — tariffs, currency, a soft UK — while building a dominant and more profitable US position. If anything, I may be slightly ungenerous with the figures I am inputting for my valuation.

There are still risks: my background concerns about consumer confidence, inflation, and Trump's propensity to do damage — for instance further tariffs on Switzerland or on other imports into the United States. I would anticipate the market taking these results in its stride, and there is a good argument for more positive feelings on where the company is heading — I would expect the shares to continue moving up. But I will stick with my core fundamentals.

Valuation

I am using the basic (statutory) earnings-per-share figure of 42.6p rather than the adjusted figure — the exceptionals have now largely washed through, and it is the more conservative base. Because I am so impressed with the growth thus far, I am applying an estimated further earnings-per-share growth rate of 10% going forwards, and — because they really have done a very good job — an 8% valuation uplift. Against that, the risk factor is a relatively high 30%, reflecting consumer confidence, inflation and tariff risk. That produces a valuation of 662.52p per share against a current price of 749.50p — a value opportunity of −11.6%.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • The margin recovery doesn't arrive: FY27's +40 to +80bp adjusted EBIT margin guidance is the key swing factor. Without it, earnings growth stays pinned below revenue growth and the earnings case becomes considerably less attractive.
  • Consumer confidence, inflation and tariffs: My background concerns — including Trump's propensity to do damage, for instance further tariffs on Switzerland or on other imports into the United States. This is why my risk factor is a relatively high 30%.
  • The takeover talks come to nothing: No formal offer has been made. If the take-private interest evaporates, the optionality premium in the 749.50p price deflates back toward fundamentals.
  • Currency: With 51% of revenue now in US dollars, a stronger pound is a material translation headwind — around £47m in FY26 alone.
  • Concentration: Watches are 82.4% of revenue, and supply of the key brands (Rolex above all) is allocated by the brands, not controlled by the retailer.

What Would Change My Mind

  • The margin recovery lands: Delivery of the guided 8.9–9.3% adjusted EBIT margin on the 52-week base would imply adjusted earnings per share of around 49–54p — at which point my 42.6p input, 10% growth and 8x multiple all start to look ungenerous.
  • A formal offer materialises: Sources suggest a price expectation well above 750p; a firm bid would crystallise value above my fair-value estimate.
  • US momentum compounds: Continued 15%+ underlying US growth at a 10%+ margin would keep shifting the mix toward the more profitable half of the business.
  • Diversification scales: If jewellery, pre-owned and ecommerce (24% of revenue) genuinely outgrow the Group as management expects, the dependence on allocated watch supply falls.

Bottom Line — Hold

These are strong results — better than the market expected before May — with the US now the majority of revenue and nearly two-thirds of profit, cash conversion at 80%, net debt down to £57m, and disciplined inventory. I am very happy with where the company is sitting, and they have navigated tariffs, currency and a soft UK admirably.

But the headlines flatter: the 53rd week added £44m of revenue, margins contracted 60bp, and roughly 85% of the statutory profit jump was exceptionals washing out. On my deliberately conservative inputs the shares are worth 662.52p against a 749.50p price — a gap largely explained by takeover optionality. FY27's margin recovery is the swing factor; until it lands, I will stick with my core fundamentals.

Hold. Value opportunity: −11.6%. Probability: 50%. Research grade: A.
