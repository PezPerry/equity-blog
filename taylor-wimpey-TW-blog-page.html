Holding Up, But Not Enough to Warrant Investment

Briefing

  • Thesis: Taylor Wimpey's trading update shows numbers that are generally holding up — sales steady, cancellations improving, order book down but not collapsing. But the net private sales rate has slipped from 0.77 to 0.74, build cost inflation is stepping up, pricing is softening, and the dividend policy is too generous for the environment.
  • Valuation view: Using a net asset value approach (appropriate for a housebuilder of this scale), starting from reported net assets of £4.19 billion and applying a 30% discount, I arrive at an implied share price of approximately 83.8p. Against a current price of approximately 83.4p, the value opportunity is just +0.5% — effectively fair value.
  • Key risk: Build cost inflation has been upgraded to "low to mid single digits" from "low single digits," with Stifel estimating sector-wide inflation could reach 4.5%. Order book pricing is down approximately 1% year-on-year. Margins will be squeezed.
  • Key catalyst: A recovery in mortgage rates or housing market confidence would boost sales rates and order book values. The short-term land bank of 76,000 plots plus a 133,000-plot pipeline provides optionality if volumes recover.
  • Overall stance: Fair value — no action. Not enough return to warrant investment at current levels.

Business Model

Taylor Wimpey is one of the UK's largest housebuilders by volume, completing approximately 11,229 homes in FY 2025 (including joint ventures). The company operates across the UK and has a small Spanish operation. It builds a range of homes from starter properties through to larger family houses, selling primarily to private buyers and housing associations.

The business model is straightforward: acquire land, obtain planning permission, build homes, and sell them. Revenue is directly linked to the number of completions and the average selling price (approximately £335,000 in FY 2025). Profitability depends on the spread between the selling price and the build cost — and both of those are currently under pressure.

🎓 Clarity Why do housebuilders get valued on net asset value rather than earnings? Housebuilder profits are highly cyclical — they boom when the housing market is strong and collapse when it weakens. This makes price-to-earnings ratios unreliable. Net asset value (the value of all the company's assets minus its liabilities) is a more stable anchor because it reflects the underlying land bank, work-in-progress inventory, and cash on the balance sheet. When a housebuilder trades at a large discount to net asset value, it often signals the market expects future losses that will erode those assets. When it trades at a premium, the market expects those assets to generate returns above their book value.

Dividend Policy — Too Generous

The company announced in March that it intends to return 5% of net assets as an ordinary dividend, with a further 2.5% of net assets returned annually by way of ordinary dividends or share buybacks.

This is too high for my liking. At a time when things aren't particularly great, and we can see elsewhere — with Crest Nicholson now struggling with banking covenants and needing more cash — it's surprising to me that Taylor Wimpey are being this generous. I would prefer greater capital conservation.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Build cost inflation accelerating: Guidance has already been upgraded once. If energy costs continue rising due to the Middle East conflict, margins will compress further from the already-declining 17.1% gross margin.
  • Sales rate decline: The net private sales rate of 0.74 is below the prior year's 0.77. If this deteriorates further, completion volumes and revenue will fall.
  • Dividend policy too generous: Returning 7.5% of net assets annually in dividends and buybacks is aggressive when margins are compressing and the macro outlook is uncertain. Capital conservation should be the priority.
  • Order book pricing pressure: Pricing is down approximately 1% year-on-year. If cost inflation rises while selling prices fall, the margin squeeze accelerates.
  • Housing market downturn: A sustained rise in mortgage rates or further deterioration in consumer confidence could reduce demand for new homes and force price cuts.
  • Crest Nicholson precedent: The sector is not without risk — Crest Nicholson's recent covenant difficulties demonstrate what can happen when housebuilders face a cash squeeze.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Net private sales rate above 0.85: A sustained improvement in the sales rate would signal genuine demand recovery and support higher completion volumes.
  • Share price below 70p: At lower prices, the discount to net asset value would widen and the 9.1% dividend yield would become even more compelling, creating a clearer margin of safety.
  • Gross margin stabilising above 20%: A return to margins above 20% (from the current 17.1%) would signal pricing power is returning and cost pressures are manageable.
  • Mortgage rate cuts: A sustained reduction in mortgage rates would directly improve affordability and boost housing demand.
  • Dividend policy reset: A more conservative distribution policy would signal management is prioritising balance sheet resilience — paradoxically, this would make the stock more attractive.

Conclusion — Fair Value, No Action

Despite this not being a particularly positive or enlightening trading update, there is some positivity to be gleaned: Taylor Wimpey is not announcing more dramatically poorer figures. Their numbers are generally holding up — sales steady, cancellations improving, order book down but not collapsing.

However, the economic backdrop isn't great — build costs are stepping up, pricing is softening, and margins will be lower. The dividend policy remains too generous for the environment.

With a value opportunity of just +0.5% and a 25% risk factor, there is not a great enough return here to warrant investment.

Value opportunity: +0.5%. Fair value — no action. Research grade B.
