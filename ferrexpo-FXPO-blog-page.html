Business Model

Ferrexpo is an iron ore pellet producer with mining operations near Kremenchuk in central Ukraine. The company is vertically integrated — it mines iron ore, processes it into pellets, and sells the finished product to steel mills, primarily in Europe (Austria, Slovakia, Czech Republic, Germany). It also operates a fleet of vessels on the Rhine and Danube waterways and holds an interest in a port in Odessa.

The company was historically a significant global producer, but the Russian invasion of Ukraine and subsequent operational disruptions have reduced output to approximately 25% of pre-war capacity. The majority shareholder is Kostyantin Zhevago, through his vehicle Fevamotinico S.à r.l., holding 49.32% of ordinary shares.

What's Driving the Crisis — VAT Refund Withholding

The Ukrainian tax authorities suspended VAT refunds to the group from March 2025, arising from the group's association with its majority shareholder, Kostyantin Zhevago (the "Mister Zhevago" issue). This withholding has continued into 2026 and to date. As of 31 March 2026, the group's net VAT receivable balance in Ukraine was approximately $90.3 million.

This has had a severe impact on the group's liquidity and forced a further downscaling in production, lower procurement, and reduced output. The Ukrainian government is effectively targeting the company because of its association with Zhevago.

🎓 Clarity What is a VAT refund and why does it matter here? When a company exports goods, it typically pays value added tax on its inputs (materials, energy, services) but charges no VAT on its exports. The government owes the company a refund for the difference. For Ferrexpo, this refund is approximately $90 million — money they are legally owed but which the Ukrainian authorities are withholding because of the company's association with Zhevago. Without this cash, the company cannot fund its operations, even though it is technically profitable on an operating basis.

The $100 Million Equity Raise

Shareholders will not be keen on participating in a $100 million equity raise — that's roughly 40% of the current market capitalisation at current prices, and would be hugely dilutive. And to be truly honest, what does it buy them? It buys approximately 18 months of breathing room for the company to get things in order, but none of the fundamental factors are actually improved:

  • The Ukrainian government is going to continue withholding VAT refunds from the company consistently and on an ongoing basis
  • The Ukrainian war is not about to end
  • Energy costs are expected to be higher than previously anticipated in the coming months
  • The company is operating at just 25% of pre-war capacity with no clear path to normalisation

It's quite bullish to try and buy shares in an equity raise under these conditions. I don't think they'll be able to achieve it. I could be wrong — perhaps there is enough willing capital out there — but I think this company is going to fold imminently, possibly at the end of the month if the fundraise doesn't complete by the 30 April deadline.

The Fevamotinico relationship agreement remains in place (the fundraise is not expected to dilute them below 24.9%), meaning the Zhevago issue persists regardless. Discussions with the largest shareholder about supporting the fundraise remain "ongoing," which is not exactly a ringing endorsement.
