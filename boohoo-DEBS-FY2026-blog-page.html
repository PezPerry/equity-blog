Margins or Sales, But Not Both

Boohoo Group, now branded Debenhams Group — mid-way through a turnaround from fast-fashion to a marketplace model. All figures in pounds sterling.

Briefing

  • Thesis: The company is mid-way through a multi-year turnaround, attempting to pivot from a stock-heavy fast-fashion model to a "stock-light, capital-light" marketplace led by the Debenhams brand. There is some progress — but it has been far too slow, and the financial damage is severe.
  • The core problem: They've proven they can sell a lot, and they've proven they can improve margins — but they haven't proven they can do both at the same time, which is the thing that actually matters. Turnover has more than halved from its peak, and net asset value has been all but wiped out.
  • Valuation view: Working forward from a recovery assumption (a 3% return on turnover in three years, ~1.28p of earnings per share), with a 7x multiple, a 20% reduction and a 39% risk factor, I arrive at a valuation of 8.17p. Against a current price of 25p, that's a value opportunity of −67.3%.
  • Some progress: Gross margin rose for the first time since 2022; the Debenhams marketplace reached 25,000 partner brands and £730m of GMV. But it's getting long in the tooth — and I've yet to see why someone would choose Debenhams over Amazon.
  • Overall stance: Sell. Research grade B. Until they can show they can sell and profit at once, the valuation doesn't support the price.

FY 2026 Headline Results

These are the figures for the year ending 28 February 2026. The company announced gross margin up 40 basis points to 51.1% — the first improvement in this area since 2022. The Debenhams brand saw an 11.6% increase in GMV and is now the largest brand in the group at £730m. Adjusted EBITDA increased 36% to £53m, comfortably ahead of guidance, driven by a 76% rise in second-half adjusted EBITDA. But this does not hold much sway with me — I feel EBITDA is a poor metric for gauging company performance.

🎓 Clarity What is GMV — and why isn't it revenue? GMV stands for Gross Merchandise Value — the total value of goods sold across the platform before deducting returns, and before the company's own "take". In a marketplace model, the retailer doesn't buy the stock; it lists third-party sellers' products and takes a commission (the "take rate") on each sale. So GMV measures the total transaction value flowing through the platform, while the company's recognised revenue is only the commission slice. This is why group revenue can fall even as GMV and profitability improve — the marketplace recognises commission income rather than the full transaction value. It's a useful gauge of platform scale, but it is not revenue, and it flatters the apparent size of the business.

Note on EBITDA: the company leads on adjusted EBITDA of £53m. EBITDA is too easily manipulated and is being largely set aside as a measure — it strips out interest, tax, depreciation, amortisation, share-based payments and exceptionals, which for a company with heavy lease and restructuring costs flatters the picture considerably.

I still don't love the Debenhams brand idea. I can see the logic — it makes sense for a group to build a network of brands around a main brand to drive better sales — and it is working for them currently. But I do have to wonder where this is all heading. It's getting slightly long in the tooth, if I'm honest. And I've yet to see why someone would realistically go to the Debenhams website rather than just Amazon. Nonetheless, it does seem to be working for them right now.

Things I Don't Like

Something I do not like: they set up their US distribution centre and then, after a very short period, closed it down. They spent years putting it together. There is a lease in place for the remaining 8.5 years, which they are subletting to ID Logistics. I suppose it's not the end of the world, but it's still not great.

On international expansion, the Debenhams marketplace has launched in the US via Macy's, Bloomingdale's, Nordstrom and Amazon Fashion. There is still a lot they really need to be figuring out. I don't think there is a huge amount in this initial statement, and I'm not going to look into the company any further right now.

The Profitability Problem

It's been a long time since this company has really made any money. Their sales are crashing, and progress on righting the ship has been slow.

I'm quite surprised, really — I used to believe they were the future of clothing retailing in the UK. They very much don't appear to be that way now. Sales are dropping dramatically (partly as they intend, cutting unprofitable lines), and while they have managed to narrow their losses, they still have a very long way to go.

Net asset value — all but wiped out (FY 2020–2025, £m)

Source: Boohoo / Debenhams Group annual reports. Net asset value (the book value of the company's equity) has collapsed from a £473m peak in FY 2021 to just £4m in FY 2025 — five years of losses eating almost the entire equity base.

The trajectory is stark. Turnover peaked at £1,983m in FY 2022 and has since collapsed to £790m — less than half. The company swung from a £92m pre-tax profit in FY 2020 to a £264m pre-tax loss in FY 2025. Net asset value has been all but wiped out, falling from a £473m peak to just £4m. The shift to marketplace explains part of the revenue fall — commission income replacing full transaction value — but the losses are significant.

Sure, they're able to improve margins, but at a ridiculously high offset to revenues, which isn't good for their long-term future. They've proven they can sell a lot, and they've proven they can hit better profitability metrics — but they haven't proven they can do both at the same time, which is quite important for a business!

Valuation

My valuation works forward from a recovery assumption. If we assume they can eventually make a profitability return on turnover of 3%, and give it another three years to get there, you'd be coming up with a profit of around £20.1m on turnover — about 1.28p per share. I apply a long-term growth rate of 2% thereafter.

I have to give it a 20% valuation reduction because of how long it will take to get there, and a 39% risk factor, because who knows where this is currently heading. The shares already discount a lot of bad news, but the uncertainty is severe.

🎓 Clarity Why value the company on assumed future earnings? Boohoo/Debenhams is currently lossmaking, so there are no real earnings to value it on today. Instead of using a current earnings-per-share figure, the valuation works forward: it assumes the turnaround succeeds enough to deliver a modest 3% profit margin on sales in three years' time (about 1.28p of earnings per share), then applies a multiple, a small growth rate, and large discounts for the time it'll take and the risk that it doesn't happen at all. It's a "give them the benefit of the doubt on recovery" valuation — and even on that generous basis, the shares still look heavily overvalued.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong (for the Sell case)

  • The turnaround actually works: If the Debenhams marketplace scales faster than expected and they hit the 3% margin sooner, the recovery valuation rises and the shares could re-rate. The main risk to a Sell.
  • Bad news already in the price: The shares already discount a lot of pain — net asset value near zero, sales halved. There may be limited further downside if sentiment is already rock-bottom.
  • Cost-base progress: The fixed-cost exit rate falling to £119m (from £175m) shows real cost discipline — if sustained, profitability could arrive faster.
  • Balance-sheet support: The £40m fundraise and net debt under 2x adjusted EBITDA give some breathing room.
  • Takeover/asset interest: A heavily depressed valuation can attract bidders or activists interested in the brand portfolio.

My View

The company is not doing well, and I can't really see where they're heading. They do seem to be righting the ship, but far too slowly. They can get margins, but at a ridiculously high cost to profitability and revenues. They've proven they can sell a lot, or hit better profitability metrics — but not both at once, which is the thing that actually matters. Until they can demonstrate that combination, the valuation doesn't support the current price.

Bottom Line — Sell

I used to believe Boohoo were the future of UK clothing retail. They very much don't appear to be that way now. Sales have more than halved, five years of losses have all but wiped out the equity, and the marketplace pivot — while showing some early signs of life — is getting long in the tooth, with no clear reason a shopper would pick Debenhams over Amazon.

Even on a generous recovery-based valuation that gives them the benefit of the doubt, the shares look heavily overvalued at −67.3%. They've shown they can sell a lot, or improve margins — but not both at once. Until that changes, the price isn't supported.

Sell. Value opportunity: −67.3%. Probability: 50%. Research grade: B.
