Business Model

Zigup (formerly Redde Northgate) is the leading integrated mobility solutions provider in the UK, Ireland, and Spain. The company offers vehicle rental, fleet management, accident management, vehicle repairs, and service and maintenance across the vehicle lifecycle. It is a FTSE 250 constituent.

The business operates through two main divisions: Rental (commercial vehicle rental and fleet management in the UK, Ireland, and Spain) and Claims & Services (accident management, vehicle repairs, and legal services). Revenue is driven by the number of vehicles on hire, utilisation rates, and the profitability of vehicle disposals when fleet vehicles are sold at the end of their useful life.

🎓 Clarity Why is the adjusted versus statutory profit gap so important here? Zigup's adjusted profit before tax was £166.9 million in FY 2025, but statutory profit before tax was only £126 million — a gap of £40.9 million. The adjustments include depreciation differences (£26.5 million), amortisation of acquired intangible assets (£18.3 million), and exceptional items (£20.6 million). While management presents the adjusted figure as the "true" picture of the business, many of these charges recur every year. Using the adjusted number would significantly overstate the company's actual earnings power.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Adjusted versus statutory gap persisting: The £40+ million gap between adjusted and statutory profit recurs every year. Until this narrows, the company's actual earnings power is lower than management presents.
  • Leverage at top of range: At 1.9x, leverage is at the upper end of the 1.0–2.0x target. Any further deterioration in earnings or increase in fleet investment could push it above the ceiling.
  • Disposal profit normalisation: Pandemic-era used vehicle price inflation is fading. As disposal profits normalise to £10–15 million per segment, a key earnings tailwind disappears.
  • Gross margin compression: Gross margin has declined from 29.2% (FY 2023) to 22.0% (FY 2025). If this trend continues, operating profit will decline even if revenue stabilises.
  • Revenue plateau: Revenue dipped 1.1% in FY 2025 after five years of growth. If organic growth does not return, the business is effectively ex-growth.
  • Cyber risk: The £4.2 million cyber incident in Claims & Services is a reminder of operational risk in a technology-dependent business.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Share price below 370p: Would widen the value opportunity to double digits and push the dividend yield above 7%, making the income case more compelling.
  • Revenue returning to growth: Sustained organic revenue growth above 3% would support a higher growth rate assumption in the valuation.
  • Adjusted to statutory gap narrowing below £20 million: Would signal that the recurring "adjustments" are genuinely reducing rather than being a permanent feature.
  • Leverage below 1.5x: Deleveraging would reduce financial risk and signal improved cash generation from the fleet.
  • Steady-state cash flow target delivered early: Achieving the £200 million+ cash flow target ahead of FY 2027/28 would validate the operational strategy.

Conclusion — No Action

On a statutory basis, the stock is marginally overvalued at −3.4%. The trading update was very light on information, and FY 2026 profit before tax is expected to be below the prior year.

Something I would say positively about the company is that even at the current share price, they're hitting a dividend yield of 6.3%, which is very good.

No Action. Value opportunity: −3.4%. Probability: 40%. Research grade: B.
