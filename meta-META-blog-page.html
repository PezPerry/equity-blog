Business Model

Meta Platforms operates the world's largest family of social media and messaging applications — Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Messenger, and Threads — reaching approximately 3.56 billion daily active people across the family of apps. The overwhelming majority of revenue comes from digital advertising: businesses pay to place targeted ads in front of Meta's enormous user base.

The company reports in two segments: Family of Apps (the advertising business, which generates essentially all the profit) and Reality Labs (the metaverse and augmented/virtual reality hardware division, which continues to lose money heavily). The investment case rests almost entirely on the advertising business and, increasingly, on how artificial intelligence is improving its efficiency.

🎓 Clarity How does Meta actually make money? Meta's revenue is almost entirely advertising. When you use Facebook or Instagram, Meta shows you ads. Advertisers pay based on two things: the number of times their ad is shown (impressions) and the price per ad (which is set by an auction). In Q1 2026, impressions grew 19% and average price per ad grew 12% — so Meta is showing more ads AND charging more for each one. Artificial intelligence improves both: better content recommendations keep users scrolling (more impressions), and better ad targeting makes each ad more valuable to advertisers (higher prices).

Financial History

It's interesting — I had not realised quite how much Meta has grown in terms of both revenue and profit over recent years. FY 2024 to FY 2025 wasn't a great profit growth year (post-tax profit actually declined from $62.4 billion to $60.5 billion), but if we compare FY 2025 back to FY 2020, post-tax profits have grown by 107% — nearly 22% per year on average over that five-year period. While FY 2025 saw earnings per share decline by 1.6%, over two years earnings per share has grown 25.7%, and over three years 39.8%. They have been growing at quite a rate.

What Matters for This Company

My general inclination is that the company can continue with an earnings per share growth rate of about 20% over the medium term. That might seem a lot, but I believe they are just at the beginning of their AI cycle — there will be much more improvement to their metrics as a result of advances in AI. What I actually expect is more volatile figures than that — sudden jumps in profitability rather than smooth compounding. They have been cutting staff numbers and implementing AI at a heavy rate. Their AI expenditure has been, and will continue to be, colossal for this and coming years.

The capital expenditure level is pretty mind-blowing — $125–145 billion for a single year.

For someone like me on the outside, I look at Meta and ask: what's new in the last few years? I'm not really aware of anything fundamentally transformative. But clearly the revenue and profit growth tells a different story — they are extracting ever more value from their existing user base through AI-driven ad efficiency.

Risks and What Could Go Wrong

  • Capex return profile: $125–145 billion of annual capital spending is an enormous bet on AI infrastructure. If the returns don't materialise, this destroys shareholder value on a colossal scale. The market's 6–7% selloff on the capex raise reflects this concern.
  • Net debt swing: Meta has gone from $6.9 billion net cash to $48 billion net debt in five years. The capex programme is now being partly debt-funded, increasing financial risk.
  • Reality Labs losses: The metaverse division continues to lose money heavily, a persistent drag that the advertising business has to carry.
  • Regulatory risk: Meta faces ongoing antitrust scrutiny in the US and EU, plus data privacy regulation that could constrain its ad targeting capabilities.
  • Single revenue stream: The business is almost entirely dependent on advertising. Any structural shift in digital advertising — or a recession that cuts ad budgets — hits Meta directly.
  • "What's actually new?": The growth is coming from squeezing more value from the existing user base rather than genuinely new products. That can continue for a while, but it isn't infinite.

What Would Change My Mind

  • Share price below $500: Would open up a clear value opportunity and provide a margin of safety against the capex risk.
  • Evidence of capex payoff: Clear demonstration that the AI infrastructure spend is driving incremental revenue and margin — not just cost — would justify a higher multiple.
  • Sustained 20%+ earnings growth: If Meta delivers the medium-term growth I expect, the current price would prove cheap in hindsight.
  • Capex discipline: Any signal that the company is moderating the capex trajectory toward returns rather than raw scale would reduce the risk factor.
  • Reality Labs narrowing losses: A path to breakeven for the metaverse division would remove a persistent drag and improve group economics.

Bottom Line — Fair Value

Meta has quietly become the most efficient ad company on earth, extracting ever more value from its 3.56 billion daily users through AI-driven ad efficiency. The growth has been remarkable — 107% profit growth over five years.

But at the current price, the stock screens as approximately fair value, and the capital expenditure programme — $135 billion at the midpoint — introduces genuine uncertainty. The market's selloff on the capex raise tells you everything: the spend is accelerating faster than confidence in the payoff.

Fair value. No action. Value opportunity: −1.8%. Probability: 50%.
