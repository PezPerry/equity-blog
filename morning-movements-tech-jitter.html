A tech jitter, not a turning point

Markets dropped sharply overnight — the NASDAQ off around 2.6% and Asia hit hard. But on my reading of valuations, this looks like a minor blip in a crowded trade, not the start of something worse.

23 June 2026 · 5 min read

We saw overnight that markets dropped quite significantly. The NASDAQ is down around 2.6% as of this moment, and the UK market is down too — this after a pretty strong rise yesterday, which came on hopes of political stability following Starmer’s resignation and Andy Burnham being seen as the shoo-in for the next prime minister. So why the sudden jitter? A NASDAQ move down of around 2.6% overnight is a significant move and worth understanding.

South Korea was the stress point

The clearest stress point overnight was South Korea. The KOSPI — the South Korean stock market — fell more than 8%, which triggered a circuit breaker and a temporary suspension of trading. It looks like an unwinding of AI exposure: the index had been one of the world’s best performers this year on the back of its semiconductor heavyweights (Samsung and SK Hynix), so it was the most crowded, most appreciated position when sentiment turned. Memory-chip names led the falls, with the two giants down around 8–12%.

This is probably driven by expectations around the path of US interest rates. After last week’s hawkish Federal Reserve meeting, traders have been scaling back any hopes of rate cuts — and some are now positioning for higher rates by year end rather than lower. Higher borrowing costs are particularly damaging for AI and tech companies, which are heavily dependent on capital expenditure. A high-growth stock doesn’t only fall when its business weakens; it can also fall simply because the discount rate applied to its future profits has gone up. That is largely what is happening here.

Sentiment can change very abruptly. But a forced unwinding of a crowded trade is not the same thing as a verdict on whether the underlying AI demand is there.

Why I am not too concerned

I’m not too worried about this recent movement. The US tech stocks I’ve looked into don’t look overpriced on their current valuations — you can see this in my company analysis for Alphabet, Microsoft and Apple, among others. They carry colossal absolute valuations, but against their current earnings power they screen as roughly fair, not wildly stretched. When the names at the centre of a sell-off aren’t obviously expensive to begin with, a sharp drop tends to look more like a positioning flush than a fundamental re-rating.

It is true that the NASDAQ has been on a upward run in recent months. The charts below show the last twelve months and the last six months — you can see the steady climb to recent highs, and today’s pullback in context.

Importantly, the futures have not moved as dramatically higher as I had anticipated, and I am no longer expecting significantly higher inflation in the US, EU or UK over the coming year as I previously feared. On that front things are actually looking surprisingly good. For that reason, I think this is a minor blip.

What I’ll do

I’ll watch to see if it drops any lower, and if it does I’d look to take the opportunity to add to my NASDAQ exposure in my trading. But overall I’m not too concerned about this cut. A crowded, highly appreciated trade being unwound on a rate scare is uncomfortable, but it is the kind of move that tends to create entry points rather than destroy them — especially when, on my own numbers, the leading names weren’t expensive going in.
